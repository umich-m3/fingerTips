Eyelid Disorders

Apraxia of Lid Opening

    • What is it?
    • Delayed opening of eyes after lid closure
    • Manifestation of central nervous system bradykinesia
    • Common causes: advanced Parkinson disease, frontotemporal dementia, Alzheimer disease
    • Uncommon causes: depression, advanced progressive supranuclear palsy, multi-infarct state, multiple system atrophy, Huntington disease, Wilson disease, idiopathic

    What does it look like?
    • Core clinical features
      • Elevation of closed lids to command is slow and delayed, but usually complete
      • Prolonged bilateral lid closure occurs spontaneously or after exposure to bright light or touch of brow Video
    • Possible accompanying clinical features
      • Patient may be able to evoke eyelid opening by prying one eye open
      • Manual attempt by examiner to open eyes may induce blepharospasm
      • Parkinsonism
      • Dementia

    What else looks like it?
    • Severe bilateral ptosis, but…
    • Tip: unlike apraxia of lid opening, ptosis does not improve after a delay
    • Blepharospasm, but…
    • Tip: Expect more marked and prolonged contraction of the orbicularis oculi in blepharospasm than in apraxia of lid opening
    • Tip: here are two other ways to distinguish apraxia from blepharospasm
      • Brows are lower in blepharospasm, reflecting contraction of the orbicularis oculi
      • Parkinsonian features are present in apraxia of lid opening, but not in blepharospasm

    What should you do?
    • Try to decide whether this is apraxia (bradykinesia) or blepharospasm, because…
    • If blepharospasm is the main component, botulinum toxin injection around eyelids may help, whereas…
    • If apraxia (bradykinesia) is the main component, carbidopa treatment may be more effective, and…
    • Trap: if bradykynesia is the main component, botulinum toxin injection may cause excessive eyelid laxity and troublesome exposure keratopathy, especially if blink rate is low
    • Reassure patients that slow opening of lids is compatible with reasonable execution of activities of daily living

    What will happen?
    • In apraxia of parkinsonian states, anti-parkinsonian agents may help
    • Even if apraxia is the main component, a component of blepharospasm is often present, which may be relieved with low-dose botulinum toxin injection
