Eyelid Disorders

Apraxia of Lid Opening

    • What is it?

    • Delayed opening of the eyes after lid closure
    • Manifestation of central nervous system bradykinesia
    • Common causes: advanced Parkinson disease, frontotemporal dementia, Alzheimer disease
    • Uncommon causes: depression, advanced progressive supranuclear palsy, multi-infarct state, multiple system atrophy, Huntington disease, Wilson disease, idiopathic

    What does it look like?

    • Core clinical features
      • Prolonged bilateral lid closure occurs spontaneously or after exposure to bright light or touch of brow
      • Elevation to command of the patient’s closed lids is slow and delayed
    • Possible accompanying clinical features
      • Patient may be able to evoke eyelid opening by prying one eye open
      • Manual attempt by the examiner to open the patient’s eyes may induce blepharospasm
      • Parkinsonism
      • Dementia

    What else looks like it?

    • Severe bilateral ptosis, but unlike apraxia of lid opening, ptosis does not improve after a delay
    • Blepharospasm, but that causes more marked and prolonged contraction of the orbicularis oculi than does apraxia of lid opening
    • Tip: here are two other ways to distinguish apraxia from blepharospasm
      • Brows are lower in blepharospasm, reflecting contraction of the orbicularis oculi
      • Parkinsonian features are present in apraxia of lid opening, but not in blepharospasm

    What should you do?

    • Try to decide whether this is apraxia (bradykinesia) or blepharospasm (active orbicularis contraction), because…
    • If blepharospasm is the main component, botulinum toxin injection around the lids may help, whereas…
    • If apraxia is the main component, carbidopa treatment may be more effective, and botulinum toxin injection may cause excessive lid laxity and troublesome exposure keratopathy, especially if the blink rate is low
    • Reassure patients that slow opening of their lids is compatible with reasonable execution of activities of daily living

    What will happen?

    • In apraxia of parkinsonian states, anti-parkinsonian agents may help
    • Even if apraxia is the diagnosis, a component of blepharospasm is often present, which may be relieved with low-dose botulinum toxin injection
