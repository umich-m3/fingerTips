Hemifacial Spasm

    • What is it?
    • Involuntary contraction of facial muscles on one side
    • Attributed to pathologic excitability of facial nerve
    • Common causes: idiopathic, compression of facial nerve from aberrant superior cerebellar artery or anterior inferior cerebellar artery
    • Uncommon cause: cerebellopontine angle or brainstem mass

    What does it look like?
    • Core clinical features
      • Facial muscles contract intermittently in unison, tightening face 
      • Upper face, including orbicularis oculi, is usually most prominently involved
    • Possible accompanying clinical features
      • Triggered or exacerbated by social interactions
    • Imaging features
      • Heavily T2-weighted MRI may show compression of root of facial nerve by aberrant vessel
    • MRI or CT may show mass in cerebellopontine angle or pons

    What else looks like it?
    • Static contracture of ipsilateral facial muscles from old facial palsy (“post-paretic facial contracture”) Video

    What should you do?
    • Consider ordering brain MRI/MRA to rule out pontine lesion or compressive lesion of facial nerve root, realizing that abnormalities will rarely be found if the hemifacial spasm is the sole manifestation
    • Tip: if hearing loss or trigeminal hypesthesia is present, brain imaging is mandatory as an intracranial mass lesion is likely
    • If imaging is negative, prescribe botulinum toxin injections into affected side of face
    • For botulinum-refractory cases, consider sub-occipital surgical decompression with placement of separator between facial nerve root and aberrant intracranial artery, even if imaging fails to show corroborative abnormalities

    What will happen?
    • Botulinum toxin injections usually relieve symptoms for 3–4 months, can be repeated, and will usually provide long term relief
    • In botulinum failures, sub-occipital surgical decompression is usually effective, but includes risk of stroke and deafness
