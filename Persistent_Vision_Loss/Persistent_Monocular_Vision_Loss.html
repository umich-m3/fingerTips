Persistent Vision Loss

Persistent Monocular Vision Loss

    • What is it?
    • Enduring visual loss affecting only one eye
    • Common causes
      • Optical disorder
      • Retinopathy
      • Optic neuropathy
      • Amblyopia
      • Psychogenic ("non-organic") disorder

    How does it appear?
    • Consider these clues to diagnosis
      • Vision impaired for distance viewing but preserved for reading = uncorrected myopia
      • Ghost image = optical (refractice or ocular media) disorder
      • Reduced vision dating from early childhood in setting of ocular misalignment, anisometropia, ptosis, congenital corneal or lens opacity = amblyopia
      • Specks that lag behind eye movement (floaters) = vitreous detachment or other vitreous debris
      • Black areas that do not lag behind eye movement = retinopathy
      • Blank areas in visual fields ("scotomas") = retinopathy, optic neuropathy
      • Sparkling vision (if monocular) = retinopathy

    What else looks like it?
    • Double vision (diplopia), but exclude this by finding that symptom is eliminated by covering either eye

    What should you do?
    • Follow These Steps
      • Step 1: measure visual acuity
      • Step 2: perform pinhole examination; if visual acuity improves visual acuity, consider uncorrected refractive error or corneal or lens opacity
      • Step 3: perform refraction and biomicroscop to confirm optical disorder; if negative…
      • Step 4: perform swinging flashlight pupil test to look for relative afferent pupil defect which would suggest optic neuropathy or extensive retinopathy; if afferent pupil defect not present…
      • Step 5: perform appropriate tests to exclude amblyopia, including detection of ocular misalignment, anisometropia, ptosis, and congenital corneal or lens opacity; if you have excluded amblyopia…
      • Step 6: perform ophthalmoscopy Ophthmaloscopy to exclude retinal or optic nerve abnormalities if diagnosis still remains uncertain…
      • Step 7: perform formal visual field examination to detect patterns of visual loss that suggest visual pathway lesions (See Visual Fields Examination Techniques); if diagnosis still remains uncertain…
      • Step 8: perform ancillary studies such as optical coherence tomography, fluorescein angiography, ultrasonography, electroretinography, orbit/brain imaging

    What will happen?
    • Most organic causes of persistent monocular visual loss should be revealed with appropriate office testing, although ancillary studies may be necessary
    • Early diagnosis may be important to improve outcomes
