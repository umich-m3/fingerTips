Multiple Ocular Motor Cranial Nerve Palsies

    • Video overview
      Ocular Motor Cranial nerve Pathways Part 1,2, and 3

    What is it?
    • More than one ocular motor nerve palsy occurring simultaneously
    • Palsies may be present in one eye only (“unilateral ophthalmoplegia”) or in both eyes (“bilateral ophthalmoplegia”)
    • In unilateral ophthalmoplegia, the lesion is often in the ipsilateral cavernous sinus/superior orbital fissure
    • In bilateral ophthalmoplegia, the lesion may be in the basal meninges, clivus, sellar region, both cavernous sinuses, or ocular motor nerves themselves
    • Common causes of unilateral ophthalmoplegia: neoplasm, aneurysm, infection, inflammation, carotid-cavernous fistula
    • Common causes of bilateral ophthalmoplegia: meningitis, clival mass, Fisher variant of Guillain-Barré syndrome, post-viral mononeuritis multiplex, bilateral cavernous sinus lesions

    What does it look like?
    • More than one ocular motor cranial nerve palsy in one eye
    • Single ocular motor cranial palsy involving both eyes

    What else looks like it?
    • Graves diesease
    • Orbital cellulitis, myositis, tumor (including sinus mucocele), trauma
    • Myasthenia gravis
    • Brainstem lesion

    What should you do?
    • Order brain/orbit MRI
    • Trap: brain MRI may be negative in ocular motor cranial nerve infection, non-infectious inflammation, and neoplastic infiltration
    • Tip: CT/CTV and MRI/MRV do not reliably detect carotid-cavernous fistula; catheter cerebral angiography is needed to show early venous filling (See Carotid-Cavernous Fistula)
    • Order catheter cerebral angiogram if you suspect carotid-cavernous fistula, but only if you decide that it requires prompt repair
    • If MRI is unrevealing, and you do not suspect carotid-cavernous fistula, order lumbar puncture
    • Trap: lumbar puncture may fail to detect meningeal cancer and indolent inflammation
    • Order CT of the chest, abdomen, and pelvis in search of a primary cancer site or multicentric disease
    • Order a biopsy if there is an accessible lesion
    • Tip: craniotomy access to a lesion in the middle cranial fossa is perilous; if there is a component of the lesion in the paranasal sinuses or nose, that will be the way to go

    What will happen?
    • If multiple cranial nerve palsies are confined to one eye, the lesion probably lies in the superior orbital fissure or cavernous sinus; biopsy via sinonasal access may be feasible and necessary
    • If multiple cranial nerve palsies involve both eyes, the lesion could be in the paranasal sinuses, cavernous sinuses, or meninges; although sinonasal access for biopsy may be appropriate, imaging and lumbar puncture are likely to be more helpful to diagnosis
