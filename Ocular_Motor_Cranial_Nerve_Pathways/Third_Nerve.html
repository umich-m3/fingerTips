Third Nerve Pathways

    • Video overview
      Video

    What is it?
    • Cranial nerve that supplies medial, superior, and inferior rectus muscles and inferior oblique muscle to provide ipsilateral adduction, supraduction, infraduction
    • Also supplies levator palpebrae superioris to provide elevation of upper lid, ciliary muscle to provide accommodation, and iris sphincter to provide pupil constriction
    • Five anatomic segments: nuclear, fascicular, subarachnoid, cavernous, orbital

    How does it work?
    • Signals originate in cluster of paramedian midbrain nuclei
      • Somatic subnuclei mediate adduction (via medial rectus), supraduction (via superior rectus and inferior oblique), infraduction (via inferior rectus), and upper lid elevation (via levator palpebrae superioris)
      • Parasympathetic (Edinger-Westphal) subnucleus mediates accommodation (via ciliary muscle) and pupil constriction (via iris sphincter muscle)
    • Axons leave third nerve nuclei and travel ventrolaterally, passing through red nucleus, substantia nigra, and cerebral peduncle, gathering as single nerve that exits brainstem in interpeduncular fossa
    • Nerve traverses subarachnoid space between posterior cerebral artery and superior cerebral artery, passing inferolaterally to junction of posterior communicating artery and internal carotid artery
    • Nerve leaves subarachnoid space to enter cavernous sinus
      in its superior outer wall
    • Nerve leaves cavernous sinus in superior orbital fissure to enter orbit; superior division supplies levator and superior rectus; inferior divison supplies medial and inferior recti and inferior oblique
    • Parasympathetic portion of nerve splits off from inferior division to synapse in ciliary ganglion;
      short posterior ciliary nerves leave ciliary ganglion to enter posterior scleral wall, eventually supplying iris sphincter (for pupil constriction) and ciliary muscle (for accommodation)

    How does it go wrong?
    • Lesion of nucleus
      • Causes ipsilateral deficits in adduction and infraduction
      • Causes contralateral deficit in supraduction (because of crossed innervation of superior rectus muscle)
      • Tip: lesion of third nerve nucleus is extremely rare!
    • Lesion of fascicles Fascicle Lesion
      • Produces complete or incomplete third nerve palsy
        Third nerve palsy patient
      • Tip: third nerve palsy often accompanied by binocular vertical gaze deficits because of proximity of riMLF and INC, which mediate vertical gaze
      • Common cause: infarct of proximal posterior cerebral artery penetrating branches to midbrain and thalamus (“top of the basilar syndrome”) Top of Basilar Top of basilar Patient
      • May be accompanied by ipsilateral or contralateral limb tremor or ataxia because fascicles pass through red nucleus and superior cerebellar peduncle Fasicle Lesion
      • May be accompanied by contralateral limb weakness because exiting fascicles pass near cerebral peduncle carrying corticopontine and corticospinal tracts Weber Syndrome
    • Lesion of subarachnoid segment
      • Produces complete or incomplete third nerve palsy
      • Tip: reversible ischemia—the most common cause of isolated third nerve palsy in adults--occurs here or in cavernous segment Third nerve palsy ischemic plus schematic
      • Tip: in ischemic lesions, pupil may be spared because ischemia affects core of nerve and pupillomotor axons lie on periphery of nerve
      • Nerve compression by aneurysm occurs at the junction of internal carotid and posterior communicating arteries,
        the apex of basilar artery, and at the take-off of superior cerebellar artery
      • Tip: in compressive third nerve palsies, including aneurysm and uncal herniation, dilated and poorly constricting pupil often occurs because compression impacts periphery of nerve, where pupillomotor axons lie, but compressive lesions may spare the pupil
      • Inflammation
        and intrinsic nerve tumors affect this segment of the nerve
    • Lesion of cavernous segment
      • Causes complete or incomplete third nerve palsy, often in combination with other ocular motor palsies, trigeminopathy, Horner syndrome
      • Common causes: pituitary tumors, intracavernous aneurysms, tumors, inflammations, and fistulas
      • Tip: cavernous lesions often damage nerve’s superior division more than its inferior division, so that ptosis and a supraduction deficit may occur selectively
        Superior Division Lesion Patient
    • Lesion of superior orbital segment
      • Produces complete or incomplete third nerve palsy, often in combination with other ocular motor palsies, trigeminopathy, Horner syndrome
      • Tip: lesions confined to the orbit, even the posterior orbit, rarely produce deficits in third nerve function because the nerve enters the proximal portions of extraocular muscles and levator; ductional deficits and ptosis from orbital lesions are more often the result of obstruction, weakening, or scarring of muscles
    • Lesion of ciliary ganglion or ciliary nerves
      • Produces ipsilateral mydriasis and impaired pupil constriction to light, but no ductionals deficits or ptosis
      • Produces slow (“tonic”) pupil constriction to a near targetand slow redilatation when gaze is redirected from near to far Tonic Pupil
      • Tonic pupil is usually idiopathic, but may be caused by orbital trauma (including surgery), inflammation, or dense retinal photocoagulation
