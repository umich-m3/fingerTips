A 35 year old man suddenly developed the inability to move his eyes to the right. He has been entirely healthy, taking no medications, and lacking any family history of neurologic disorders. You find that his eyes have normal sight, appear structurally normal, but do not move beyond mid position to the right, even with the doll’s eye (“oculocephalic”) maneuver. There are no other neurologic abnormalities. For example, vertical eye movements are completely normal.

    • Review Topic

    Where is the lesion?

    A) Right cerebral hemisphere

    Incorrect

    B) Left cerebral hemisphere

    Incorrect

    C) Right pons

    Correct!
    This patient has a palsy of right horizontal conjugate gaze (“gaze palsy”) that involves saccades, pursuit, and the vestibulo-ocular reflex. You could postulate a lesion affecting muscles or the neuromuscular junction. But such a symmetrical deficit of horizontal conjugate gaze to one side will virtually always be caused by a lesion in the pons. With a lesion in the pons, you might expect to find other neurologic deficits--seventh nerve palsy, internuclear ophthalmoplegia, nystagmus, ataxia, or limb weakness. However, if the lesion is confined to a small area in the location of the sixth nerve nucleus, you might not find any such accompanying signs (although you should look for them as “fellow travelers”). This patient had a hemorrhage from a right pontine tegmental cavernous malformation (“cavernoma”).
    • The only effective treatment is surgical excision, which is hazardous. No treatment was undertaken and his deficits gradually resolved enough to give him partial restoration of right gaze. He was advised to avoid any activity that would generate increased intracranial pressure--like weight-lifting, evacuative straining, and standing on his head!
