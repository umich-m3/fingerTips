A 15 year old boy notices that when he looks upward, his eyes ache and that he gets double vision. You find that with attempted upward gaze, his eyes do not move upward, but instead they converge!

    • Review Topic

    The responsible lesion lies in the

    A) Posterior commissure

    Correct!
    This is an important neuro-ophthalmic landmark! Lying at the junction of the thalamus and midbrain, it mediates upward gaze by connecting the RIMLFs and INCs on both sides.
    A lesion here SELECTIVELY interferes with upward gaze, sparing downward gaze, which is mediated more ventrally. When this commissure is damaged by an intrinsic lesion of the caudal thalamus or pretectal or tectal midbrain regions, or by an extrinsic lesion like a pinealoma, expect elements of a “dorsal midbrain syndrome.” You may have heard it called the “pretectal syndrome,” “Sylvian aqueduct syndrome,” “Parinaud syndrome” (if you are French), or “Koerber-Salus-Elschnig syndrome” (if you are German). The elements of this syndrome include convergence retraction, lid retraction (“Collier’s sign”), dilated pupils with light-near dissociation, and perhaps esotropia, exotropia, or hypertropia (skew deviation or fourth nerve palsy). This patient had a pinealoma.
    • Early diagnosis favors a better outcome of treatment. By the way, even with early diagnosis of pineal tumor, some features of a dorsal midbrain syndrome—especially upgaze deficiency and convergence retraction--often linger. Why is this fact important? Because if upgaze deficiency or convergence retraction is detected long after treatment of the tumor, do not take that as a sign of tumor recurrence or of a complication of radiotherapy. Either of those signs could have been overlooked when the tumor was first diagnosed!
