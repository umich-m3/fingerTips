A 24 year old man suffers severe head trauma from a fall. Weeks later, he reports lingering diplopia to his caregivers at a rehabilitation center. Your examination shows a comitant esotropia with full ocular ductions. He is wheelchair-bound. He has saccadic pursuit and gaze-evoked horizontal nystagmus, lingering cognitive impairment, ataxia of speech and extremities, and spastic quadriplegia

    • Review Topic

    Where is the lesion causing the esotropia?

    A) Diencephalon

    Correct! Although this patient has suffered severe damage to all parts of his brain, the comitant esotropia with full ocular ductions suggests a breakdown of the balance between convergence and divergence. The vergence pathway that controls that balance could certainly be damaged in its cerebral hemispheric segment, but that was not one of your choices here. The diencephalon and subjacent mesencephalon are important centers for vergence. Other answers will not work here. Bilateral sixth nerve palsy is a common mistaken explanation, but that should reduce abduction and cause more esotropia on side gaze than in primary gaze position (“incomitant esotropia”). Bilateral third nerve palsy would cause an exotropia and affect ocular ductions, the position of the lids and the size and reactivity of the pupils. Bilateral fourth nerve palsy would cause a right hypertropia in left gaze and a left hypertropia in right gaze, features not present here.
