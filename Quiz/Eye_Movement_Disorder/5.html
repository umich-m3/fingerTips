A 54 year old woman is brought to you for anisocoria. As you can see, the left pupil is larger than the right pupil. The right pupil does not constrict to light; the left pupil constricts normally.

    • Review Topic

    Which maneuver should you next perform to help with the diagnosis?

    A) Swinging light test for afferent pupil defect

    Incorrect

    B) Assessment of right pupil constriction to a near target

    Correct! Whenever a pupil does not constrict properly to direct light, you should assess whether it constricts to a target placed within reading distance. Why? Because if that pupil constricts reasonably well to a near target, you have diagnosed “light-near dissociation,” a phenomenon of great clinical value. It signifies a lesion in one of three locations: 1) ipsilateral optic nerve or retina; 2) dorsal midbrain/pretectum; 3) ipsilateral ciliary ganglion or ciliary nerves. You will need additional clinical information to make the distinction between these three lesion locations.
