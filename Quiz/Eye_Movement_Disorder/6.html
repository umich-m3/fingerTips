A 24 year old medical student is troubled because her roommate noticed that she has anisocoria. Both pupils constrict briskly and equally to direct light. There are no other abnormalities.

    • Review Topic

    Which medication should you instill in the eyes to deliver a useful explanation?

    A) Apraclonidine 0.5%

    Correct! Because both pupils constrict normally to light, there are only four possible causes: 1) physiologic anisocoria; 2) episodic mydriasis; 3) topical adrenergic-induced mydriasis; 4) Horner syndrome. If apraclonidine instillation reverses the anisocoria, you have diagnosed Horner syndrome, which can exist even without ipsilateral ptosis (or ipsilateral hypohidrosis)! If apraclonidine does not reverse the anisocoria, you are left with the other three possibilities, none of which is medically important. Now if the patient tells you that she has used a nasal spray or eyedrop to “get the red out,” you can diagnose adrenergic mydriasis. The diagnosis of episodic mydriasis will have to wait until the pupils become equal in size by the next morning. That condition is sometimes associated with migraine, seizure, and other manifestations of a dysautonomia. Physiologic anisocoria is the last—but most common-- possibility. In that case, you can confidently reassure the patient that the condition is benign, that the anisocoria will often come and go, and that the larger pupil may even jump from one eye to the other! By the way, topical instillation of cocaine 10% was formerly the standard way to test for a Horner syndrome, but it has been displaced by apraclonidine, which is more reliable and available. However, you must use topical cocaine 10% in children aged 2 years or younger because apraclonidine may cause dangerous cardiopulmonary effects. Topical pilocarpine tests are used in the diagnosis of anisocoria when either pupil fails to constrict to light.
