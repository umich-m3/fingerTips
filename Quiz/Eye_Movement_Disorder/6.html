A 24 year old medical student is troubled because her roommate noticed that she has anisocoria. Both pupils constrict briskly and equally to direct light. There are no other abnormalities.

    • Review Topic

    What maneuver could you perform that would deliver a useful explanation?

    A) Instillation of apraclonidine 0.5% eyedrop onto each eye

    Correct! Because both pupils constrict normally to light, there are only four possible causes: 1) physiologic anisocoria; 2) episodic mydriasis; 3) topical adrenergic-induced mydriasis; 4) Horner syndrome. If apraclonidine instillation reverses the anisocoria, you have diagnosed Horner syndrome, which can exist even without ipsilateral ptosis (or ipsilateral hypohidrosis)! If apraclonidine does not reverse the anisocoria, you are left with the other three possibilities, none of which is medically important. Suppose that the patient tells you that she has used a nasal spray or eyedrop to “get the red out,” then you can implicate adrenergic mydriasis. You will be able to diagnose episodic mydriasis if the pupils have become equal in size by the next morning. Precipitants of episodic mydriasis are migraine, seizure, application of topical anticholinergic agents for axillary hyperhydrosis, and manifestations of a dysautonomia. Physiologic anisocoria is the last possibility. You could expect that the patient would have noticed the anisocoria earlier, but it too can be intermittent, but not exceed 2mm.
