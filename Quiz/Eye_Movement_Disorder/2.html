A 2 year old boy is sent to you because his pediatrician has noticed that the patient’s eyes “are crossed.” Your examination discloses a comitant esotropia when the child views distant and near targets. Ocular ductions are full and there is no nystagmus. To the extent that it is possible to examine a 2 year old, the ophthalmic and neurologic examinations are otherwise normal, including a plano refractive error.

    • Review Topic

    A possible cause of this abnormality is

    A) Sixth nerve palsy

    Incorrect

    B) Accommodative esotropia

    Incorrect

    C) Infantile esotropia

    Correct! If your observation is correct—that the right eye is “turned in,” that there are full ocular ductions, and that the misalignment is of the same amount in right and left gaze—then the abnormality is not likely to be a sixth nerve palsy, which should produce an incomitant esotropia with greatest amount in ipsilateral gaze. Yes, sixth nerve palsy can eventually develop full ductions and become a comitant esotropia, but that would be highly unusual in a 2 year old child. Unilateral optic neuropathy could produce a comitant esotropia by interrupting fusion, but you should find an afferent pupil defect unless the lesion affects both optic nerves equally (or affects only the optic chiasm). Accommodative esotropia is ruled out by the plano refraction.

    D) Optic neuropathy from pilocytic astrocytoma of the right optic nerve (“optic glioma”)
