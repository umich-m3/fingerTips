C) Petrous apicitis

Correct! The abnormality here is a complete left abduction deficit. Given the new left ear pain, there are two likely causes: petrous apicitis and sigmoid sinus thrombosis producing high intracranial pressure. Either process can emanate from middle ear infection, diagnosed by inspection and by imaging, which showed fullness of the left mastoid region. The absence of papilledema was a vote against high intracranial pressure from sigmoid sinus thrombosis, as was the lack of headache. Also, CT venography failed to show thrombosis. Why the ipsilateral sixth nerve palsy? Because the sixth nerve passes through Dorello’s canal, a tight space adjacent to the petrous apex. Inflammation probably spreads via venous channels. Anti-infectives and mastoidectomy were curative for this potentially life-threatening condition also known as Gradenigo’s syndrome. Another cause of sixth nerve palsy from mastoiditis is sigmoid sinus thrombosis. In that condition, the palsy might be caused by increased intracranial pressure. The patient would manifest papilledema.
