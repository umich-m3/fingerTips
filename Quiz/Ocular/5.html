D) Pinealoma

Correct! You are looking at convergence retraction of the eyes on attempted upward gaze. The pinealoma
    • presses on the dorsal midbrain and, more importantly, the posterior commissure, which carries important connections for upward gaze. As upward gaze fails, the brain misfires, activating all extraocular muscles and pulling the eyes backward into the orbit. Because the medial muscles are the strongest extraocular muscles, the eyes also converge. This bizarre eye movement causes pain. Any lesion in the adjacent thalamus (mostly stroke) or midbrain (demyelination, tumor) can cause this phenomenon, as can obstructive hydrocephalus from aqueductal stenosis or ventricular shunt malfunction. This oscillation is not actually a nystagmus because it is not initiated by a conjugate slow phase drift. Rather, it is a form of clonus. Convergence-retraction is never an isolated phenomenon; look for impaired upgaze, reduced pupil constriction to light (often with preservation of constriction to a near target), esotropia, or exotropia.
