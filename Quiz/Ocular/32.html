A 55 year old man complained of new pain on the right side of his neck and noted a droopy right upper lid when he looked in the mirror.

Review Topic

What caused the right pupil to go from being the smaller pupil at the beginning of the video to being the larger pupil at the end of the video?

B) Topical instillation of apraclonidine

Correct! The combination of ptosis, miosis, and ipsilateral neck pain suggests Horner syndrome from carotid dissection. As the signs of Horner syndrome are subtle (and ptosis and miosis could have separate causes), it is critical to confirm the diagnosis pharmacologically. In patients aged over 2 years, the agent of choice is apraclonidine 0.5%. A positive test is reversal of the anisocoria, so that the formerly small pupil becomes the larger one, as happened here. Topical cocaine, used in children under age 2 because apraclonidine may cause cardiopulmonary problems, provides a less discrete endpoint. A positive topical cocaine test for Horner syndrome is residual anisocoria of at least 1mm. Although useful in dilating pupils in the regular ophthalmic examination, tropicamide and phenylephrine have no diagnostic role in this setting.
