This 55 year old man complains of chronic foreign body sensation and redness of both eyes, as well as droopy eyelids, which have been present for as long as he can remember. In addition to what you see here, the patient also had very weak orbicularis oculi function, which led to ocular exposure.

What condition is causing these manifestations?

A) Progressive supranuclear palsy

Incorrect

B) Chronic progressive external ophthalmoplegia

Correct! The key abnormalities are bilateral ptosis and slow, reduced ocular ductions in all directions in both eyes, together with conjunctival hyperemia. The pan-ductional deficits could certainly be caused by progressive supranuclear palsy, but that condition would not cause ptosis or weak orbicularis oculi function. Moreover, the dollâ€™s eye maneuver--a critical maneuver in diagnosing a supranuclear gaze deficit--did not improve eye movement here. Pan-ductional slow eye movements can also occur in spinocerebellar ataxias, but that condition does not cause ptosis, and ataxia would be prominent. Myasthenia gravis could cause these ocular movement abnormalities, ptosis, and orbicularis weakness, but would rarely produce enough exposure to cause such red eyes. These abnormalities are the result of a nuclear-mediated mitochondrial disorder called chronic progressive external ophthalmoplegia. Because all extraocular muscles are often equally weakened, ocular alignment may be normal for distance viewing, but convergence will be deficient, producing exotropia and diplopia at reading distance. There is no direct treatment.
