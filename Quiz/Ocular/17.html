This 62 year old man reports that his vision becomes blurred and jiggly when he moves. The standard ophthalmologic and neurologic examinations are normal.

Ocular 17

Review Topic

What is this?

A) Vestibulo-ocular reflex dysfunction

Correct!You are looking at normal ocular pursuit movements, but the patient has an abnormal head impulse test and an abnormal dynamic visual acuity test. In the head impulse test, when the examiner grasps the patient’s head and moves it briskly to one side and then to the other side, the eyes make contraversive saccades. If the vestibulo-ocular reflex (VOR) were working normally, that head movement should elicit an equal and opposite shift of the eyes, so that the eyes would remain in the fixated position. But if the VOR is impaired bilaterally, the eyes will be carried eccentrically with the head movement and will automatically move back toward center when the head stops moving. In the dynamic visual acuity test, which is not part of the standard ophthalmologic examination either (visual acuity is always tested with the patient’s head still), the back-and-forth eccentric movements of the eyes interrupt fixation, so visual acuity falls. These abnormalities trace to dysfunction of the inner ear structures designed to maintain fixity of the eyes in space when the head or body moves. The most common cause is aminoglycoside toxicity. This patient had been treated with gentamicin for a serious wound infection. Patients also complain of imbalance, especially when walking on poorly-lit and uneven surfaces. The deficits are permanent, but patients usually say that their symptoms lessen over time, perhaps because they avoid the inciting circumstances.
