C) Thalamus

Correct!The lids are up and the eyes are down. Side gaze is reasonably intact. This combination of abnormalities--if acute--often localizes to the thalamus. An expanding lesion there, either a hemorrhage or an infarct, puts pressure on the posterior commissure, which lies immediately caudal to the thalamus. That commissure carries the pathway for upgaze. When it is damaged, the eyes cannot move up. If the damage is severe, the eyes will deviate downward--the “sunset sign.” Lid retraction (“Collier’s sign”) is common with lesions in this region; its mechanism is unsettled. Another important cause of the sunset sign is sudden obstructive hydrocephalus and malfunction of a ventricular shunt placed for obstructive hydrocephalus. The preserved horizontal gaze tells you that neural signaling is getting through to the pons.
