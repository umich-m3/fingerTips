A 65 year old woman reports that for the past year, she has noticed vertical diplopia from time to time, especially when she is tired. The episodes of diplopia are becoming more frequent.

Review Topic

What is causing them?

A) Decompensated congenital fourth nerve palsy

Correct! You probably recognized that the hypertropia obeys two of the steps of the “three-step test,” namely that there is a left hypertropia greater on right gaze than left gaze. The third step in the “three-step test”—the comparison of the size of the hypertropia in right head tilt and left head tilt—was not performed. But you do see the “fourth step”—the comparison of the size of the hypertropia in upgaze and downgaze. In this patient, the hypertropia is greater in upgaze than in downgaze. That feature excludes an acquired fourth nerve palsy from trauma, tumor, or ischemia, and suggests a decompensated congenital “palsy.” If you had found excyclodeviation on double Maddox rod testing (the “fifth step”), you would have gone a long way toward excluding myasthenia gravis or a tight inferior rectus on the side of the greatest hypertropia produced by chronic inflammation (including Graves disease). Relief of the diplopia might happen with prism glasses, but is more likely with eye muscle surgery. If you are able to diagnose a decompensated fourth nerve palsy, brain imaging is not necessary.
