A 35 year old man reports that he notices vertical diplopia from time to time, especially when he is tired. The episodes of diplopia are becoming more frequent.

Ocular 24

Review Topic

What is causing them?

A) Myasthenia gravis

Incorrect

B) Decompensated superior oblique muscle dysfunction

Correct! You probably recognized that the hypertropia obeys the “three-step test,” which is strongly suggestive of a fourth nerve palsy—here on the left. But knowing the cause of this pattern of misalignment depends also on noting that the hypertropia is greater in upgaze than in downgaze. Such a feature suggests three possible causes: a decompensated superior oblique muscle dysfunction, myasthenia gravis, or a tight inferior rectus on the side of the greatest hypertropia produced by trauma, surgery, or chronic inflammation (including Graves disease). You would need the results of the Double Maddox Rod test to confirm a diagnosis of a decompensated superior oblique muscle. It should show excyclodeviation, an abnormality that would be unusual in myasthenia or an orbital lesion. Relief of the diplopia might happen with prism glasses, but is more likely with eye muscle surgery. If you are able to diagnose a decompensated superior oblique muscle dysfunction, brain imaging is not necessary.
