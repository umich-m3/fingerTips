Correct! Why no more testing? You are observing an abduction deficit in the right eye and esotropia, but—more importantly—a narrowing of the palpebral fissure on adduction of the affected (right) eye. This is a congenital disorder called Duane syndrome, a dysplasia of the ipsilateral sixth nerve nucleus in which the neurons innervating the lateral rectus muscle are deficient. As a result, abduction is impaired and the eyes are esotropic. One clue to the diagnosis is the chronicity of the symptom and the fact that diplopia is only occasional, probably because the condition has been present since birth, allowing suppression of the image from the deviating eye. The clinching clue is the narrowing palpebral fissure height on adduction, perhaps the result of aberrant innervation of the lateral rectus muscle by axons of the third nerve, such that the medial and lateral rectus muscles co-contract on adduction, pulling the eye backwards into the orbit. If diplopia or a marked face turn is present, surgical weakening of the medial rectus may be effective.
