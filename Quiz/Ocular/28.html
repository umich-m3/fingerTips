This 28 year old man has had hooding of the eyes and diplopia for two weeks. He has suffered no illnesses and takes no medications. There is no pertinent family history. All aspects of the ophthalmologic and neurologic examinations are normal, including normal size and constriction of the pupils and intact deep tendon reflexes.

Review Topic

What is causing these abnormalities?

A) Myasthenia gravis

Correct! There is obvious bilateral ptosis. The eyes do not move completely in many directions. You might have missed an important clue to myastheniaâ€”the quivering movements of the eyes. In myasthenia, saccades start out at normal speed, but then abruptly stop when acetylcholine in the synaptic space has linked to the limited number of intact neuromuscular junction receptors. That phenomenon can also occur when eye movements are restricted by tight extraocular muscles, but orbital restrictive conditions, including Graves disease, would not cause profound ptosis. You noticed that the ductional deficits are not symmetric between the eyes, so this is not a gaze palsy, as might be seen with cerebral, diencephalic, or brainstem lesions. Pontine lesions could certainly cause asymmetric horizontal eye movement deficits, but would not cause ptosis and would usually spare vertical eye movements. Bilateral damage to multiple ocular motor cranial nerves (cancer, inflammation) could certainly cause this pattern. If you were thinking of the Fisher variant of Guillain-Barre syndrome (polycranial neuritis), you would look for absent deep tendon reflexes. The patient had a positive serum acetylcholine receptor antibody. A combination of pyridostigmine, a cholinesterase inhibitor, and prednisone (which reduces the autoimmune destruction of neuromuscular junction cholinergic receptors), completely relieved the ptosis and restored full eye movements and normal ocular alignment.
