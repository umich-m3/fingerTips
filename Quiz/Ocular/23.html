A 17 year old man describes vertical diplopia that began many months ago. He has had no other symptoms or known medical problems. He takes no medications.

Ocular 23

Review Topic

What caused these abnormalities?

A) Fourth nerve palsy

Correct! This patient has a right hypertropia that increases in amplitude in left gaze. The hypertropia is greater in right head tilt than in left head tilt. These abnormalities constitute a positive “three-step test,” which compares the amplitude of the hypertropia in lateral gazes and head tilts. The most likely diagnosis is right fourth nerve palsy. You might also expect ocular misalignment in the torsional plane, assessed with the Double Maddox Rod test. In fact, he displayed 5 degrees of excyclodeviation on that test. Also important—and shown here—is that the hypertropia is worse in down-and-left gaze than in straight left gaze, a sign that the fourth nerve palsy is acquired. Among the causes at this young age are a caudal dorsal midbrain lesion (tumor, multiple sclerosis), inflammation and tumor of the subarachnoid segment of the nerve, and—most commonly—head trauma. (In older adults it would be extra-axial ischemia and trauma.) Actually, shortly before the onset of diplopia, this young man had fallen on the ice and sustained brief unconsciousness after striking his head. He did not think it relevant, so he did not mention it to his examiners. By the way, had the hypertropia been of greater amplitude in up-and-left gaze than in down-and-left gaze, a secret alternative diagnosis would have been more likely.
