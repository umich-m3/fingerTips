What is the diagnosis?

A) Spasm of the near reflex

Incorrect

B) Bilateral sixth nerve palsy

Incorrect

C) Myasthenia gravis

Incorrect

D) Convergence substitution in horizontal gaze palsy

Correct!
This bizarre eye movement disturbance is actually quite common in patients with absent horizontal gaze. Convergence is involuntarily substituted, so that the eyes are drawn toward one another. In this patient, that phenomenon is more prominent when she attempts leftward gaze. The cause was a large pontine glioma.
    • In spasm of the near reflex, horizontal gaze is intact, although it may be interrupted by convergence (and miosis and accommodation). This patient does have a left sixth nerve palsy (which causes the esotropia), but that condition alone does not evoke the convergence movements you see here. Myasthenia gravis, if it impaired horizontal gaze completely, might cause substituted convergence, but that would be unusual.
