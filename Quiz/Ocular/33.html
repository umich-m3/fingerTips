B) Tonic (Adie) pupil

Correct! The right pupil does not constrict to direct light; the left pupil constricts normally. The right pupil constricts slowly when the patient shifts gaze from a distant to a near target; the left pupil constricts briskly with that gaze shift. The right pupil dilates slowly when the patient shifts gaze from a near to a distant target; the left pupil dilates briskly with that gaze shift. The dissociation between absent constriction to light and intact (although slow) constriction to a near target is called “light/near dissociation.” The slow constriction is called “tonic constriction.” Notice also that the right pupil is not perfectly round; a magnified view would reveal that the slow constriction is not occurring evenly, a feature known as “segmental constriction” or “stromal streaming.” The combination of these abnormalities spells a diagnosis of tonic (Adie) pupil, attributed to denervation from a lesion in the ciliary ganglion or ciliary nerves. The cause is probably a limited autoimmune disorder. Young patients may have accommodative deficiency when the attack starts, but accommodation generally recovers. You may have been fooled by the fact that the abnormal pupil is the smaller one in this patient. Do not be fooled. At outset, the affected tonic pupil is usually larger than the unaffected pupil, but with time it often becomes the smaller pupil, as it did here. This condition is frequently misdiagnosed, prompting patient fears and all sorts of unnecessary (and expensive) tests. The defect is permanent, and often the other pupil may become involved.
