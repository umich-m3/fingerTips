Correct! Oddly, this is a setting in which imaging can be deferred to allow spontaneous resolution. Why? Because at this patientâ€™s age, the palsy is overwhelmingly likely to be caused by reversible damage to the extra-axial sixth nerve from ischemia. Of course, you must be sure that there are no pertinent historical or examination features (such as cancer, an immune-compromised state, manifestations suggestive of giant cell arteritis) that would impel prompt evaluation, including imaging. In their absence, you might still argue that acute phase reactants should be drawn to help exclude giant cell arteritis and that brain imaging should be done to rule out lesions of the cavernous sinus, clivus, or sphenoid sinus. Unlike the situation with isolated third nerve palsy, there is almost no concern for intradural aneurysm. Intracranial hypertension would be signaled by papilledema and symptoms and intracranial hypotension, an often overlooked cause, would be rare unless there had been a preceding lumbar puncture or epidural anesthesia procedure. In any of these rare conditions, a short delay in diagnosis is unlikely to be harmful. So you could defer imaging to allow for the expected recovery of the palsy in 6 to 12 weeks. If that does not occur, or if new manifestations develop, you would have to order imaging and other appropriate evaluation.
