Pupil Disorders

Horner Syndrome

    • What is it?
    • Miosis, ipsilateral ptosis, and sometimes ipsilateral reduced facial sweating (“anhidrosis”) caused by lesion in oculosympathetic pathway
    • Oculosympathetic pathway is divided into central, pre-ganglionic, and post-ganglionic segments
    • Common causes of a central lesion (hypothalamus to high thoracic spinal cord): dorsolateral medullary infarction (Wallenberg syndrome),
      hypothalamic lesion
    • Common causes of pre-ganglionic lesion (thoracic spinal cord to superior cervical ganglion): paraspinal or chest tumors
    • Common causes of post-ganglionic lesion (superior cervical ganglion to eye): internal jugular vein cannulation, cervical carotid dissection,
      thyroid or other neck tumor, ear infection, cavernous sinus lesion, cluster headache

    What does it look like?
    • Core clinical features
      • Anisocoria no greater than 2.5mm in dim light Horner Syndrome
      • Both pupils constrict normally to bright light
      • Ptosis on side of smaller pupil
      • Trap: Horner syndrome can occur without ptosis—and rarely without anisocoria!
      • Affected pupil dilates less promptly than unaffected pupil when lights suddenly dimmed (“dilation lag”)
      • Trap: “dilation lag” is extremely difficult to observe without pupillometry
    • Possible accompanying clinical features
      • Ipsilateral lower lid elevation (“upside down ptosis”)
      • Ipsilateral conjunctival hyperemia
      • Ipsilateral facial anhidrosis
      • Trap: facial anhidrosis occurs only in central or pre-ganglionic lesions—and then is rarely observed without special tests!
    • Possible imaging features
      • Cervical carotid dissection
      • Neck or thoracic mass
      • Dorsolateral medullary infarction
      • Hypothalamic lesion

    What else looks like it?
    • Physiologic anisocoria
    • Episodic mydriasis
    • Traumatic, inflammatory, dysplastic iridoplegia
    • Exposure to instilled or aerosolized adrenergic or anticholinergic agents (“pharmacologic mydriasis”)
    • Ptosis from trauma, senescence, or myasthenia gravis

    What should you do?
    • Confirm diagnosis of Horner syndrome with topical pharmacologic testing
      • Instill apraclonidine 0.5%
        in both eyes; wait 30 minutes and note whether affected pupil becomes larger than unaffected pupil (“reversal of anisocoria”), confirming diagnosis of Horner syndrome
      • Trap: topical apraclonidine 0.5% elevates lid in normal subjects, so diagnosis of Horner syndrome depends entirely on reversal of anisocoria
      • Trap: apraclonidine-induced reversal of anisocoria may not occur if Horner syndrome occurred less than 48 hours earlier
      • In children aged under 24 months, instill cocaine 10% (to avoid life-threatening cardiopulmonary side effects of apraclonidine), wait 30 minutes to see if >1mm residual anisocoria, confirming diagnosis of Horner syndrome
    • If non-ophthalmic features allow localization of lesion, concentrate imaging appropriately
    • For acute isolated Horner syndrome, perform CT/CTA or MRI/MRA designed to detect cervical carotid artery dissection
    • For non-acute isolated Horner syndrome, perform CT/CTA or MRI/MRA between upper thoracic region and skull base designed to detect carotid artery dissection but also masses in chest or neck
    • Tip: lesions of neck and carotid canal may cause isolated Horner syndrome; lesions in cavernous sinus, diencephalon, and brainstem do not cause Horner syndrome without also causing neurologic deficits

    What will happen?
    • Horner syndrome often persists even if underlying lesion goes away
    • About 50% of isolated Horner syndrome cases are of undetermined origin
    • Tip: topical apraclonidine 0.5% may be prescribed safely to relieve ptosis of Horner syndrome as an alternative to lid-lifting surgery
