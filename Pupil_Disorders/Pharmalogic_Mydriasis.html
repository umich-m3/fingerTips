Pharmacologic Mydriasis

    • What is it?
    • Dilated (mydriatic) pupil attributed to topical contamination with anticholinergic or strong sympathomimetic (adrenergic) agent
    • Common causes: eyedrops, scopolamine patch, aerosols in ventilator regimens
    • Uncommon causes: atropinic vegetable substances, glycopyrrolate cream for hyperhidrosis, nasal decongestants

    What does it look like?
    • Dilated pupil in one eye or both eyes with reduced or absent constriction to light Pharmacologic Mydriasis
    • Impaired accommodation in affected eye among patients aged less than 45 years (anticholinergic effect on ciliary muscle)

    What else looks like it?
    • Tonic (Adie) pupil
    • Traumatic, inflammatory, dysplastic iridoplegia
    • Mydriasis of third nerve palsy
    • Episodic mydriasis

    What should you do?
    • Elicit history of exposure to topical contaminants
    • Assess accommodation in young patients, looking for expected reduction in affected eye (anti-cholinergic agents only)
    • Note absence of other pertinent abnormalities
    • Instill pilocarpine 1% in both eyes, wait 30 minutes, and confirm that affected pupil has not constricted
    • Tip: orally or parenterally administered medications with anticholinergic properties do not cause anisocoria and uncommonly cause mydriasis

    • Trap: accommodation will be preserved if mydriasis was caused by a powerful adrenergic agent

    What will happen?
    • When offending agent is discontinued, pupil returns to normal size and constricts normally to light within hours to days
