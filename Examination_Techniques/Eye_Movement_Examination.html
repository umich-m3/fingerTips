Additional Examination Techniques

Eye Movement Examination

    • What is it?
    • Assessment of binocular fixation and conjugate (“versional”) eye movements

    What should you do?
    • Assess fixation
      • Patient fixates your stationary light or finger
      • Look for spontaneous eye movements that take eyes off fixation (See Nystagmus and Nystagmus-like Oscillations)
    • Assess ocular pursuit
      • Patient follows your moving finger or light in all gaze directions
      • Check amplitude and smoothness of eye movements and look for oscillations in extremes of gaze
    • Assess saccades
      • Patient looks sequentially to right, left, up, and down at your stationary light or finger displayed eccentrically
      • Check latency, speed, and accuracy of eye movements
    • Assess vestibulo-ocular reflex (VOR)
      • If ocular pursuit and saccades are deficient, rotate patient’s head slowly back and forth horizontally and vertically (“Doll’s head maneuver”)
      • Look for slow conjugate eye movements in direction opposite to head movement
      • If saccades and pursuit appear intact, but patient reports blurred vision or oscillopsia with head movement, perform the following
        • Head impulse test Head Impulse Test
          • Grasp patient’s head and move it briskly horizontally to the right and then to the left, looking for corrective versional saccades opposite in direction to head movement
        • Dynamic visual acuity test Dynamic Visual Acuity Test
          • Instruct patient to read smallest identifiable line on Snellen chart with head stationary
          • Move patient’s head briskly back and forth, looking for decline in visual acuity relative to that achieved with head still
    • Perform occlusive direct ophthalmoscopy to evoke nystagmus caused by peripheral vestibular lesion
      • During direct ophthalmoscopy, look for oscillations of optic fundus
      • Cover other eye and note whether oscillations appear or increase in amplitude
    • Perform optokinetic nystagmus test
      • Hold optokinetic strip or drum at reading distance and rotate in horizontal plane to right and then to left; then rotate strip or drum in vertical plane, first upward, then downward
      • Assess eye movements in all directions in horizontal and vertical planes
    • Perform Dix-Hallpike (Nylen-Barany) maneuver in patient who reports positional vertigo
      • Grasp patient’s head and bring patient from sitting to supine position, lowering head over edge of examining table, first with head positioned straight down, then with head moved to right and left
      • Look for evoked nystagmus
    • Order caloric irrigation test if you need a more sensitive test of vestibulo-ocular function
      • Performed only in vestibular laboratory
      • Patient is placed in supine position with head elevated 30 degrees
      • Tympanum is inspected to make sure it is intact
      • Cold water is instilled into the external ear canal
      • Eye movements are recorded, usually with video goggles

    What does it mean?
    • Impaired fixation
      • Movements that take eyes away from fixation indicate inattention, nystagmus, or saccadic intrusions
    • Impaired pursuit
      • Jerky (“saccadic,” “cogwheel”) pursuit indicates central nervous dysfunction, but is not localizing
      • Trap: pursuit will appear jerky if patient is inattentive, uncooperative, or obliged to follow a target moving faster than 30 degrees/second
    • Impaired saccades
      • Slow, delayed, reduced, or inaccurate saccades derive from many kinds of central nervous system disorders
      • Unidirectional deficit of saccades suggests brain stem dysfunction
    • Impaired saccades and pursuit but intact vestibulo-ocular reflex
      • If congenital and accompanied by head thrusts, suggests congenital ocular motor apraxia
      • If acquired and chronic, suggests progressive supranuclear palsy
      • If acquired and acute, suggests bilateral cerebral hemispheric ischemia (acquired ocular motor apraxia)
    • Abnormal head impulse test
      • Conjugate refixational saccades opposite in direction from brisk head movement suggest impaired vestibulo-ocular reflex
      • If head impulse is positive only in one direction, consider diagnosis of unilateral peripheral vestibulopathy
      • If head impulse is positive in both directions, consider diagnosis of bilateral peripheral vestibulopathy
    • Abnormal dynamic visual acuity test
      • Visual acuity decline of at least 2 Snellen lines with head motion suggests bilateral peripheral vestibulopathy
    • Abnormal occlusive direct ophthalmoscopy
      • Nystagmus that develops or worsens after covering opposite eye suggests peripheral vestibular lesion
    • Abnormal optokinetic nystagmus test
      • Convergence-retraction eye movements evoked by downwardly moving stripes suggest dorsal midbrain dysfunction
      • Relatively reduced nystagmus amplitude of one eye suggests reduced innervation of extraocular muscle normally recruited in direction of fast phase
      • Jerk nystagmus with fast phase in direction of moving stripes suggests infantile nystagmus syndrome (“reversal of optokinetic nystagmus”)
      • Relatively reduced conjugate horizontal nystagmus amplitude in one gaze direction suggests Ipsilateral parietal lesion
      • Lack of any evoked nystagmus suggests organic vision loss or marked inattention
    • Abnormal Dix-Hallpike (Nylen-Barany) maneuver
      • Upbeat torsional nystagmus with fast phase toward dependent ear suggests posterior semicircular canal benign positional vertigo
      • Downbeat nystagmus suggests cerebellar disease
      • Other nystagmus patterns do not differentiate between brainstem, end-organ, and vestibular nerve dysfunction
    • Abnormal caloric irrigation test
      • Lack of evoked horizontal conjugate eye movement indicates severe dysfunction of brainstem or vestibular nerve
      • Relatively reduced amplitude of oppositely-directed jerk nystagmus indicates labyrinthine or vestibular nerve dysfunction on side of reduced amplitude
