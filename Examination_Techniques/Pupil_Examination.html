Additional Examination Techniques

Pupil Examination

    • What is it?
    • Measurement of pupil size and pupil constriction to light stimulation and to viewing a target placed within 3 inches of eye

    What should you do?
    • Step 1: assess size of pupils in dim illumination
      • Position light below chin and shine light on upper face, using minimum amount of light needed to judge pupil size
      • Measure pupil size in both eyes with gauge on Snellen near card
    • Step 2: assess constriction of pupils to direct light
      • Position light 3 inches below right eye and shine it directly on that eye; perform same maneuver on left eye
      • Note pupil size at maximal constriction to light
      • If either pupil does not constrict normally to light, test for “light-near dissociation” by comparing pupil constriction to light and target held within 3 inches of eye
    • Step 3: hunt for relative afferent pupil defect Afferent pupillary test
      • Shine light first on right pupil, then on left pupil, moving light stimulus across nasal bridge
      • Look for relatively reduced constriction or dilation of one pupil as light is shined on it
      • Trap: relative afferent pupil defect need not display dilation; small relative afferent pupil defect may be diagnosed if there is consistent difference in pupil dynamics on this test
    • Step 4: if there is anisocoria and both pupils constrict normally to light, you must rule out Horner syndrome by instilling apraclonidine 0.5% in each eye, even if there is no ptosis ipsilateral to smaller pupil
      • Look for reversal of anisocoria after 30 minutes
      • Trap: instilling apraclonidine in children aged 2 years or less may cause acute cardiovascular side effects; use cocaine 10% instead

    What does it mean?
    • Physiologic anisocoria: pupil size difference of 1mm or less, both pupils constricting normally to light, and topical apraclonidine test is negative
    • Pathologic anisocoria: there are 7 causes
      • Horner syndrome: lesion in oculosympathetic pathway allows both pupils to constrict normally to light, but affected pupil dilates poorly in darkness, and there is mild ipsilateral ptosis; apraclonidine test causes reversal of anisocoria
      • Third nerve palsy: lesion in pre-ganglionic nerve segment causes dilated, poorly constricting pupil, along with ocular ductional deficits and/or ptosis of varying severity
      • Dorsal midbrain syndrome: lesion in midbrain tectum causes large pupils that constrict poorly to light and better to near target, and some combination of vertical gaze impairment, lid retraction, and clonic convergence-retraction on attempted upgaze
      • Tonic (Adie) pupil: ciliary ganglion or ciliary nerve lesion causes slow segmental iris constriction to near target, slow dilatation on refixation at distance, and pupil constriction to dilute pilocarpine; considered a limited dysautonomia
      • Pharmacologic mydriasis: exposure to topical anticholinergic agent causes mydriatic pupil to fail to constrict to pilocarpine at 0.5% concentration or higher; topical sympathomimetic agents can also cause mild pharmacologic mydriasis, and affected pupils may not constrict normally to direct light
      • Iris sphincter lesion: iris damage from eye surgery, trauma, uveitis, or dysplasia causes affected pupil to be irregular and to constrict poorly to light; slit lamp examination usually discloses iris pathology
    • Relative afferent pupil defect: there are 3 causes
      • Ipsilateral optic neuropathy or relatively greater ipsilateral than contralateral optic neuropathy
      • Ipsilateral extensive retinopathy or relatively greater ipsilateral than contralateral retinopathy
      • Contralateral optic tract lesion
    • Light-near dissociation: pupil constricts poorly to non-fixated direct light and more amply to light fixated at 3-inch distance; there are 3 causes
      • Ipsilateral optic neuropathy (“afferent light-near dissociation
      • Ipsilateral ciliary ganglion/ciliary nerve lesion (“tonic light-near dissociation”) Tonic (Adile) Pupil
      • Dorsal midbrain syndrome (“tectal light-near dissociation”) Tectal Pupils
