Color Vision Examination

    • What is it?
    • Assessment of color (chromatic) vision

    What should you do?
    • Instruct patient to identify items in standard pseudoisochromatic color plate sets
    • Score as a fraction: Correct answers over total plates presented
    • Trap: pseudoisochromatic plate testing is designed for congenital rather than acquired color vision disorders and tends to be insensitive in assessing acquired non-cone retinocortical lesions
    • Tip: red stimulus comparison test is more effective than pseudoisochromatic plate test in detecting non-cone retinocortical lesions
      • For detecting asymmetric optic neuropathy, display red bottle cap sequentially before each eye and assess whether red stimulus appears brighter in one eye
      • For detecting hemianopic defects, display red bottle caps simultaneously on either side of vertical meridian and assess whether red stimulus appears brighter in one hemifield

    What does it mean?
    • Pseudoisochromatic plate errors may signify congenital color vision deficiencies or acquired cone dystrophies or optic neuropathies
    • Consistent interocular difference in brightness of red stimulus suggests optic nerve lesion on side where “washed out” color is perceived
    • Consistent difference in brightness of red stimulus across hemifields suggests lesion in hemifield where “washed out” color is perceived
