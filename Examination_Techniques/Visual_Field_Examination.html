Visual Field Examination

What is it?
  • Assessment of peripheral (non-foveal) achromatic vision
  • Complements testing of visual acuity, which assesses central (foveal) achromatic vision (See Visual Acuity Examination)

What should you do?
  • Visual Fields Part 1
  • Perform confrontation visual field testing to screen for large defects
  • Perform Amsler Grid Test to screen for small defects near fixation
  • Perform bowl perimetry, the most sensitive method to detect visual field defects
  • Interpret results of bowl perimetry as follows Visual Fields Part 2
  • Step 1: Get an overview of the defect pattern from GRAY SCALE PLOT
  • Step 2: Check RELIABILITY INDICES to see if patient paid attention
    • Fixation losses: how often patient signaled awareness of the stimulus when it was projected into the physiologic blind spot, where it should not have been seen; must not exceed 20%.
    • False positive responses: how often patient signaled awareness of stimulus when stimulus was not shown; must not exceed 30%.
    • False negative responses: how often patient failed to signal awareness of stimulus when it was shown above a previously established luminance threshold; must not exceed 40%.
    • Trap: reliability indices do not tell you whether the patient deliberately--and consistently--performed poorly.
  • Step 3: check actual visual field luminance thresholds on RAW DATA PLOT
  • Step 4: compare luminance thresholds to age-matched normal subjects
  • Step 5: check TOTAL DEVIATION PLOT to see how likely a normal subject would have earned those thresholds
  • Step 6: check PATTERN DEVIATION PLOT, which highlights focal defects by lifting clusters of high threshold points out of background; focal defects result from retinocortical visual pathway lesions
  • Step 7: Examine pattern of clusters of high-threshold points to determine if they are non-localizing defects, nerve fiber bundle defects, or hemianopic defects Visual Fields Part 3
  • Step 8: decide among nerve fiber bundle defects
    • Central or centrocecal scotomas (damage to the papillomacular bundle); usually caused by optic nerve lesion, but central scotoma often macular lesion
    • Arcuate or altitudinal scotomas (damage to the bundles that originate in the temporal retinal bundles that arch over the papillomacular bundles to enter the superior and inferior poles of the optic disc); usually caused by optic nerve lesion, but could be inner retinal lesion
    • Temporal wedge scotomas (damage to the retinal bundles that originate in the nasal retina and travel radially into the nasal portion of the optic disc); usually caused by congenital optic nerve dysplasia
  • Step 9:
    • Bitemporal hemianopia (temporal hemianopic defects in both eyes); caused by optic chiasm lesion, usually mass
    • Unilateral temporal hemianopia in one eye and normal visual field in the other eye (damage to optic nerve as it approaches optic chiasm); usually caused by mass lesion or inflammation
    • Temporal hemianopia in one eye and nerve fiber bundle defect in other eye (damage to optic nerve at its junction with optic chiasm); usually caused by mass
    • Complete homonymous hemianopia (damage anywhere within retrochiasmal visual pathway); usually caused by stroke, but could be tumor
    • Incomplete homonymous hemianopia (damage in retrochiasmal visual pathway with exact location and cause depending on the following specifics)
      • Incongruous homonymous hemianopia: defects not of same size and depth in two eyes; signifies optic tract lesion; usually caused by mass but could be inflammation
      • Congruous homonymous hemianopia: defects same size and depth in two eyes; signifies posterior optic radiations or visual cortex lesion; usually caused by mass
      • “Pie-in-the-sky” defects: one defect border aligned to vertical meridian and other defect border extending radially into superior visual field; signifies anterior temporal lobe (Meyer’s loop) lesion; usually caused by temporal lobectomy, but could be mass
      • Superior homonymous quadrantanopia: defect borders aligned to vertical and horizontal meridians in inferior visual field; signifies superior primary visual cortex lesion; usually caused by stroke
      • Inferior homonymous quadrantanopia: defect borders aligned to vertical and horizontal meridians in inferior visual field; signifies superior primary visual cortex lesion; usually caused by stroke
      • Homonymous paracentral scotomas: defects restricted to central 10 degrees of visual field; signify posterior primary visual cortex lesion; usually caused by stroke
      • Macular-sparing homonymous hemianopia: defects spare central 10 degrees of visual field; signifies intermediate primary visual cortex lesion; usually caused by stroke
      • Temporal crescent-sparing homonymous hemianopia: defects spare peripheral 30 degrees of temporal field of one eye; signifies intermediate and posterior visual cortex lesion; usually caused by stroke
      • Hourglass homonymous hemianopia: defect produces hourglass-shaped congruous homonymous hemianopia; signifies lesion of contralateral lateral geniculate body; caused by occlusion of anterior choroidal artery
      • Hourglass-sparing homonymous hemianopia: defects spare hourglass region; signifies lesion of lateral geniculate body; caused by occlusion of lateral posterior choroidal artery
    • Temporal crescent defect: defect limited to unpaired temporal field between 60 and 90 degrees eccentric to fixation in one eye; signifies contralateral far anterior primary visual cortex lesion; usually caused by stroke
    • Tip: the temporal crescent defect is the only monocular and nonhemianopic retrochiasmal visual field defect!
  • Step 10: recognize the 7 causes of constricted fields Visual Fields Part 4
    • Improper procedure
      • Defects consist of high thresholds around edge of field
      • Usually due to wrong refractive correction, improper placement of the lens correction, remote positioning of the patient’s head
      • Redo the visual fields, correct technical errors, clarify instructions to patients and encourage alertness to stimuli
      • If field still constricted, test wider field by confrontation, kinetic perimetry
    • Reduced palpebral fissure height
      • Defects consist of high thresholds around edge of field
      • Due to ptosis, dermatochalasis, blepharospasm, blepharophimosis
      • Repeat visual field with upper lid taped up
    • Patient inattention
      • Defects show constriction without borders of localizing value (see other causes, below)
      • Usually due to dementia, sleepiness, attention deficit disorder, other encephalopathy
      • Precipitated by poor disengagement of attention from fixation target
    • Extensive optic neuropathy
      • Defects display steps across nasal horizontal meridian (“nasal steps”)
      • Most common in glaucoma, ischemic optic neuropathy, post papilledema optic neuropathy, optic neuritis
    • Bilateral visual cortex lesions
      • Defects display steps across nasal vertical meridian with macular sparing (“keyhole fields”)
      • Defects are congruous
      • MRI usually reveals obvious lesions, usually effects of infarction
    • Retinal dystrophy
      • Defects appear as incomplete rings
      • Defects usually centered between 20 and 40 degrees eccentric to fixation
      • Full extent of defects may not appear unless perimetry assesses field beyond 30 degrees
      • No step-offs along horizontal or vertical meridian
      • Ophthalmoscopy often shows retinal pigmentary abnormalities
      • Electroretinography shows characteristic abnormalities of outer retinal degeneration
    • Deliberate non-cooperation by patient
      • Any constriction pattern may appear
      • Clover leaf pattern common
      • Progressive narrowing of visual field as kinetic perimetry proceeds (“spiraling”)
      • On confrontation testing, visual fields do not expand as testing distance increases (“tunnel field”)
      • Exclude procedural and “organic” causes
