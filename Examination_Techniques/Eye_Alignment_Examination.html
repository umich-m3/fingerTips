Examination Techniques

Eye Alignment Examination

    • What is it?
    • Assessment of alignment of eyes

    What should you do?
    • Step 1: Assess amplitude of horizontal and vertical pursuit and saccadic movements, which might give clue to ocular misalignment (See Eye Movements Examination)
      • Trap: Even if the amplitude of eye movements is normal, you must measure alignment because it may be abnormal
    • Step 2: Measure alignment with cover test
      • Trap: Cover Test will fail if patient is unable to make fixational movements because of poor vision, severely impaired eye movements, or poor understanding that target must always appear distinct
    • Step 3: If patient reports diplopia, use Single Maddox Rod Test in addition to Cover Test (or instead of Cover Test) to measure alignment Single Maddox Rod Test
      • Trap: Single Maddox Rod Test will fail if patient is unable to see red line with one eye and white fixation light with other eye
    • Step 4: If patient is reporting vertical diplopia, use Double Maddox Rod Test to assess torsional alignment Double Maddox Test
    • Step 5: If patient cannot cooperate with Cover Test or Maddox Rod tests, estimate eye alignment crudely by observing corneal light reflections

    What does it mean?
    • Comitant eye misalignment means that the degree of misalignment does not vary with gaze position; incomitant eye misalignment means that the degree of misalignment varies with gaze position
      • Comitant eye misalignment with diplopia: caused by breakdown of brain’s ability to maintain ocular alignment (“fusional force”) because of poor vision, brainstem lesion, high intracranial pressure, sleepiness, sedative medication, meningoencephalitis, aging, or convergence weakness
      • Incomitant eye misalignment with diplopia: caused by extraocular myopathy, neuromuscular transmission failure, ocular motor cranial nerve palsy, internuclear ophthalmoplegia, or skew deviation
      • Comitant or incomitant misalignment without diplopia: means either that misalignment was present at birth or in early childhood, so that image from deviating eye is suppressed, or that patients have such poor vision in at least one eye that they will not have diplopia despite eye misalignment, or that patients do not appreciate diplopia because the images are minimally and very widely separated, or that they are unable to express diplopia because of language or cognitive deficiency
