Oscillations of Internuclear Ophthalmoplegia

    • What is it?

    • Oscillations of the abducting eye in internuclear ophthalmoplegia
    • Not nystagmus because the oscillations are probably initiated by an overshoot movement of the abducting eye in the face of adduction weakness of the contralateral eye
    • Identical oscillations may occur if the adduction weakness in the contralateral eye is caused by myasthenia gravis or other condition (“pseudo internuclear ophthalmoplegia”)
    • Patient may report blurred vision or diplopia on side gaze or a vague disturbance of vision

    What does it look like?

    • Core clinical features
      • Oscillations of the abducting eye with a fast phase in the direction of gaze
      • Usually limited to extreme horizontal gaze
      • Affects one eye in unilateral internuclear ophthalmoplegia and both eyes in bilateral internuclear ophthalmoplegia
      • Always accompanied by an adduction deficit in the contralateral eye, but…
      • Trap: the adduction deficit in the contralateral eye may be mild and overlooked if eye movements are elicited by testing pursuit, therefore…
      • Tip: you should test horizontal saccadic movements by observing refixation movements or by using the optokinetic strip or drum, which elicits repetitive conjugate saccades, allowing you a better comparison of the adducting saccadic velocity in one eye to the abducting saccadic velocity in the other eye
    • Possible accompanying clinical features
      • Saccadic pursuit
      • Skew deviation
      • Exotropia
      • Upbeat or downbeat nystagmus
      • Ataxia
    • Imaging Features
      • MRI may be normal or show a lesion in the midbrain, pons, or medulla

    What else looks like it?

    • Myasthenia gravis
    • Any other condition that causes weakness of adduction
    • Tip: conditions that cause restriction (rather than paresis) of adduction typically do not elicit contralateral ocular oscillations

    What should you do?

    • Recognize this oscillation as a common—but not obligatory—accompaniment of internuclear ophthalmoplegia
    • Realize that an identical oscillation occurs in myasthenia gravis and in other conditions that cause weakness of adduction in the contralateral eye

    What will happen?

    • Oscillations will disappear if the underlying cause goes away
