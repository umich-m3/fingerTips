Nystagmus-like Oscillations

Superior Oblique Myokymia

    • What is it?
    • Episodic intorsional saccades in one eye
    • Attributed to involuntary contractions of ipsilateral superior oblique muscle
    • Triggered by abnormal excitation of fourth nerve or instability of superior oblique muscle membrane
    • Usually idiopathic and isolated

    What does it look like?
    • Core clinical features
      • Patient reports episodic twitching sensation in affected eye and simultaneous blurred or double vision lasting for seconds to hours
      • Symptoms coincide with small repetitive intorsional movements of symptomatic eye Superior Oblique Myokymia
      • Tip: intorsional eye movements best detected with magnification of slit-lamp biomicroscope
      • Trap: abnormal movement is present in only one eye; if present in both eyes, call it nystagmus
    • Imaging features
      • None

    What else looks like it?
    • Pure torsional nystagmus Torsional Nystagmus
    • Peripheral vestibular nystagmus Vestibulopathy no audio
    • Gaze-evoked (sidebeat, downbeat, upbeat) Sidebeat Nystagmus
    • Acquired pendular nystagmus Acquired Pendular Nystagmus
    • Tip: unlike superior oblique myokymia, these imitators all involve abnormal oscillations of BOTH EYES, although sometimes of asymmetric amplitude

    What should you do?
    • Listen for unique description of symptom
    • Use slit-lamp biomicroscope to find characteristic intorsional movements of affected eye during symptomatic periods
    • Try to provoke abnormal eye movements by having patient look downward
    • Brain imaging is not necessary
    • Gabapentin, carbamazepine, baclofen, or propranolol may be effective, but spontaneous remissions are so frequent that medication efficacy is not rigorously proven

    What will happen?
    • Spontaneous long-term remissions are common, but myokymia may recur without an apparent reason

    • Trap: eye muscle surgery may cause persistent diplopia, so avoid it
