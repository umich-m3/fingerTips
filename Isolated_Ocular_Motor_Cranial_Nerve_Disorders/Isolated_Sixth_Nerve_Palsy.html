Isolated Ocular Motor Cranial Nerve Disorders

Isolated Sixth Nerve Palsy

    • Video overview
      Play Video:

    What is it?
    • Unilateral lesion of the sixth nerve without other pertinent abnormalities (“isolated”)
    • Lesion is usually located in the extra-axial portion of the sixth nerve
    • Common causes: ischemia, head trauma
    • Uncommon causes: increased intracranial pressure, decreased intracranial pressure, tumor, inflammation, carotid-cavernous sinus aneurysm or fistula, Duane syndrome

    What does it look like?
    • Core clinical features
      • Blurred vision, diplopia +/- ipsilateral periocular pain
      • Ipsilateral abduction deficit, often with a floating abduction saccade in the affected eye
      • Tip: the patient may choose to fixate with the better-seeing eye, which might be the palsied eye
      • Tip: as it leaves the subarachnoid space to enter the cavernous sinus, the sixth nerve passes through Dorello’s canal, which lies between the petrous apex laterally and clivus and sphenoid sinus medially; lesions there can easily cause a sixth nerve "compartment syndrome"; here is an example
      • Tip: sixth nerve palsy may also result from downward displacement of the brain in intracranial hypertension or intracranial hypotension, which exert tension on the nerve where it is tethered in Dorello’s canal (“sixth nerve tether syndrome”)
    • Imaging features
      • Extra-axial ischemic sixth nerve palsies do not show up on high-definition MRI
      • Compressive and inflammatory lesions are best visualized with high-definition MRI centered on the cranial base
      • Trap: lesions in the clivus, sphenoid sinus, and petrous apex need not be expansive to cause sixth nerve palsy
      • Imaging signs of intracranial hypotension
        • Enlarged dural venous sinuses
        • Congested pachymeninges and pituitary gland
        • Downward displacement of the brainstem

    What else looks like it?
    • Myasthenia gravis 
    • Graves disease
    • Orbital myositis, tumor, trauma
    • Spasm of the near reflex
    • Ocular neuromyotonia

    What should you do?
    • Tip: remember that sixth nerve palsy is not the only cause of an abduction deficit; other causes are myasthenia gravis, orbital restrictive syndrome, spasm of the near reflex, and ocular neuromyotonia
    • Even if there are no obvious ductional deficits, you must perform ocular alignment testing on all patients because sixth nerve palsy can manifest as incomitant esodeviation worse in ipsilateral gaze
    • Look out for other pertinent neuro-ophthalmic features, which would make the palsy “non-isolated” and evoke different management (See Non-isolated Ocular Motor Cranial Nerve Disorders)
    • Look out for Duane syndrome, which is congenital and non-progressive; diplopia is rare and work-up is unnecessary (See Duane Syndrome )
    • Order brain MRI under the following circumstances
      • In children: order the study if they lack a history of preceding severe head trauma
      • In adults: order the study if they do not have an arteriosclerotic profile, if the palsy worsens, if the palsy has not resolved within 3 months of onset, or if additional pertinent neurologic or systemic features appear
    • Perform lumbar puncture in children and adults if brain MRI is unrevealing and the palsy does not resolve within 3 months of onset or evolves after 2 weeks of onset

    What will happen?
    • Extra-axial ischemic sixth nerve palsies should resolve completely within 3 months
    • Extra-axial non-ischemic sixth nerve palsies or fascicular ischemic sixth nerve palsies may take longer to resolve or persist indefinitely
    • For non-resolving incomplete sixth nerve palsies, horizontal eye muscle surgery usually provides a wide zone of single binocular vision
    • For non-resolving complete sixth nerve palsies, vertical extraocular muscle transposition surgery must be performed to restore normal alignment in primary gaze position, but abduction will not be restored in the affected eye
    • For neuromyotonia, oral carbamazepine may be helpful
