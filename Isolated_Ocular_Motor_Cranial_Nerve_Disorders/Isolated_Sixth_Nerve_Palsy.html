Isolated Ocular Motor Cranial Nerve Disorders

Isolated Sixth Nerve Palsy

    • Video overview
      Fourth Nerve Pathway

    What is it?
    • Unilateral lesion of sixth nerve without other pertinent abnormalities (“isolated”)
    • Lesion usually located in extra-axial portion of nerve
    • Common causes: ischemia, head trauma
    • Uncommon causes: increased intracranial pressure, decreased intracranial pressure, tumor, inflammation, carotid-cavernous sinus aneurysm or fistula, Duane syndrome

    What does it look like?
    • Core clinical features
      • Blurred vision, diplopia +/- ipsilateral periocular pain
      • Ipsilateral abduction deficit, often with a floating abduction saccade in the affected eye Sixth Nerve Palsy
      • Tip: the patient may choose to fixate with the better-seeing eye, which might be the palsied eye
      • Tip: when the palsied eye is covered, the non-palsied eye will move outward briskly and broadly to pick up fixation (“primary deviation”); when the non-palsied eye is covered, the palsied eye will move outward less briskly and broadly (“secondary deviation”) Sixth Nerve Palsy With Primary and Secondary Deviation
      • Trap: there may be no ductional deficit, so you must perform ocular alignment testing in subtle cases, looking for incomitant esodeviation worse in ipsilateral gaze Incomitant Esotropia
    • Imaging features
      • Extra-axial ischemic sixth nerve palsies do not show up on high-definition MRI
      • Compressive and inflammatory lesions are best visualized with high-definition MRI centered on the cranial base
      • Trap: lesions in the clivus need not be expansive to cause sixth nerve palsy and are frequently overlooked
      • Tip: intracranial hypertension and intracranial hypotension are uncommon--but important and often overlooked--causes of “false localizing” sixth nerve palsy; imaging signs of intracranial hypotension include
        • Enlarged dural venous sinuses
        • Congested pachymeninges and pituitary gland
        • Downward displacement of brainstem

    What else looks like it?
    • Myasthenia gravis 
    • Graves disease
    • Orbital myositis, tumor, trauma
    • Spasm of near reflex
    • Ocular neuromyotonia

    What should you do?
    • Tip: remember that sixth nerve palsy is not the only cause of an abduction deficit; other causes are myasthenia gravis, orbital restrictive syndrome, spasm of the near reflex, ocular neuromyotonia
    • Look out for other pertinent neuro-ophthalmic features, which would make the palsy “non-isolated” and evoke different management (See Non-Isolated Ocular Motor Cranial Nerve Disorders)
    • Look out for Duane syndrome, which is congenital and non-progressive; diplopia is rare and work-up is unnecessary (See Duane Syndrome )
    • Order brain MRI
      • In children at time of diagnosis if they lack history of preceding severe head trauma
      • In adults at time of diagnosis if they do not fulfill arteriosclerotic profile
      • In adults if palsy worsens or has not resolved within 3 months of onset or in whom additional pertinent neurologic or systemic features appear
    • Perform lumbar puncture in children and adults if brain MRI is unrevealing and the palsy does not resolve within 3 months of onset or evolves after 2 weeks of onset
    • Tip: sixth nerve palsy can result from downward displacement of the brain in intracranial hypertension or intracranial hypotension, which exert tension on the nerve as it enters Dorello’s canal (“sixth nerve tether syndrome”) Intracranial Hypertension and Hypotension
    • Tip: because Dorello’s canal is a tight space that lies between the clivus and the sphenoid sinus on one side and the petrous apex on the other side, disease in those regions easily causes sixth nerve palsy (“sixth nerve compartment syndrome”) Dorello's Crnal Lesion Gradenigo

    What will happen?
    • Extra-axial ischemic sixth nerve palsies should resolve completely within 3 months
    • Extra-axial non-ischemic sixth nerve palsies or fascicular ischemic sixth nerve palsies may take longer to resolve or persist
    • For non-resolving incomplete sixth nerve palsies, horizontal eye muscle surgery usually provides a wide zone of single binocular vision
    • For non-resolving complete sixth nerve palsies, vertical extraocular muscle transposition surgery must be performed to restore normal alignment in primary gaze position, but abduction will not be restored in the affected eye
    • For neuromyotonia, oral carbamazepine may be helpful
