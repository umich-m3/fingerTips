Isolated Ocular Motor Cranial Nerve Disorders

Isolated Fourth Nerve Palsy

    • Video overview
      Third Nerve Pathway

    What is it?
    • Unilateral lesion of third nerve without other pertinent abnormalities (“isolated”)
    • Lesion usually located in extra-axial portion of third cranial nerve
    • Common causes: ischemia, head trauma
    • Uncommon causes: aneurysm, pituitary apoplexy, inflammation, neoplasm, congenital

    What does it look like?
    • Core clinical features
      • Diplopia, ptosis +/- ipsilateral periocular pain
      • Tip: periocular pain can be severe and more troubling to patient than diplopia or ptosis
      • Reduced adduction, supraduction, or infraduction of affected eye 3rd Palsy Patient
      • Normal or dilated pupil with reduced constriction to light
      • Trap: most third nerve palsies have partial deficits of ocular ductions and ptosis, and many spare the pupil
    • Possible accompanying clinical features
      • Palpebral fissure that narrows on abduction and widens on adduction and infraduction, indicating “aberrant regeneration” of axons damaged by chronic compression or old trauma Traumatic Third Cranial Nerve Palsy
      • Episodes of deficient third nerve function alternating with episodes of excessive third nerve function, reflecting a congenital extra-axial third nerve lesion (“oculomotor paresis with cyclic spasms”)
    • Imaging features
      • In aneurysmal third nerve palsy, CTA or MRI usually shows responsible lesion (>95%)
      • In pituitary apoplexy, imaging usually shows enlarged pituitary gland, often with hemorrhage
      • In other causes, imaging is variably sensitive

    What should you do?
    • Tip: keep in mind that internuclear ophthalmoplegia and myasthenia are excellent imitators of third nerve palsy, but even a suspicion of third nerve palsy should trigger prompt vascular imaging in order not to overlook brain aneurysm
    • Tip: pituitary apoplexy likes to cause third nerve palsy, which may the only clinical manifestation apart from pain
    • Look for other pertinent neuro-ophthalmic features that would suggest a “non-isolated” third nerve palsy and require completely different management (See Non-isolated Ocular Motor Cranial Nerve Disorders)
    • Look for features of aberrant regeneration, which would eliminate the common ischemic cause and favor a disruptive cause such as compression, infiltration by neoplasm, or past trauma Isolated Third Nerve Palsy with Aberrant Regeneration
    • Trap: do not blame the following isolated manifestations on third nerve palsy
      • Isolated impairment of adduction (it is more likely to be caused by internuclear ophthalmoplegia, myasthenia gravis, orbital lesion, Duane syndrome type 2)
      • Isolated dilated, poorly constricting pupil (it is caused by an ocular or orbital lesion)
      • Isolated ptosis (it is likely to be myasthenia gravis, Horner syndrome, orbital lesion, or age-related degeneration of the levator aponeurosis) (See Ptosis)
      • Eye gets temporarily stuck in abduction after looking in that direction (it may be ocular neuromyotonia, a neurogenic “cramp” in lateral rectus muscle most often induced by radiation treatment of cranial base lesions)
    • Promptly order CT/CTA (substitute MRI/MRA in children and pregnant women) to rule out brain aneurysm because
      • Aneurysm cannot be excluded on the basis of clinical features alone
      • Aneurysm rupture may be imminent and fatal
      • Non-invasive vascular imaging is over 95% sensitive to the presence of an aneurysm large enough to cause a third nerve palsy
      • Early aneurysm clipping, coiling, or stenting may be life-saving
    • Trap: you will need an experienced interpreter of imaging studies to detect intracranial aneurysm
    • If imaging is negative in adults who fulfill arteriosclerotic profile, diagnose ischemic third cranial nerve palsy and intensify arteriosclerotic risk reduction measures
    • If imaging is negative in adults who do not fulfill arteriosclerotic profile, perform MRI and, if necessary, lumbar puncture
    • If imaging is negative in children, you could defer further testing and wait for resolution of presumed post-infectious cause

    What will happen?
    • Extra-axial ischemic palsy usually does not progress beyond 2 weeks and resolves spontaneously within 3 months
    • Palsy caused by compression, head trauma, or inflammation may not resolve
    • Palsy following aneurysmal compression resolves in 50% to 75% of cases, but which type of treatment provides the highest chance of recovery remains uncertain
    • In lingering diplopia, prisms and extraocular muscle surgery do not reliably produce a satisfactory zone of single binocular vision
    • Patients with persistent diplopia may be obliged to occlude one eye with a patch, spectacle occluder [ Wrap-around Spectacle Occluder or opaque contact lens Opaque Contact Lens
