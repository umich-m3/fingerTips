Vestibulo-Ocular Reflex

Video Overview
    Video

What is it?
  • Brain pathway that maintains fixation on visual target by moving eyes in response to rapid head or body movement or tilt
  • Pathway confined to brainstem
  • Under reflex rather than volitional control

How does it work?
  • Head or body movement or head tilt triggers signal in semicircular canals and otoliths
  • To generate horizontal vestibulo-ocular movements
    • Signal goes from inner ear to vestibular nerve to medial vestibular nuclei and then through medial longitudinal fasciculus (MLF) and other tegmental pathways to reach appropriate third and fourth nerve nuclei
  • To generate vertical vestibulo-ocular movements
    • Signal goes from inner ear to vestibular nerve to medial vestibular nuclei and then through medial longitudinal fasciculus (MLF) and other tegmental pathways to reach appropriate third and fourth nerve nuclei
  • To generate torsional vestibulo-ocular movements
    • Signal goes to riMLF and INC and then to appropriate third and fourth nerve nuclei
    • For example, if you incline your head to right, signal goes to right fourth nerve nucleus and left inferior rectus subnucleus, which tort eyes counterclockwise; your right intorting eye moves up while your extorting eye moves down to maintain parallel alignment to horizon

How does it go wrong?
  • Unilateral dysfunction of semicircular canals, otolith, or vestibular nerve
    • Jerk nystagmus, often with fast component in direction opposite to side of lesion
    • Unilaterally positive head impulse test (head moved to side of lesion)
  • Bilateral dysfunction of semicircular canals, otolith, or vestibular nerves
    • Eyes move in direction of head or body movement, causing blurred vision from inability to maintain ocular fixation; this impaired visual acuity with head movement can be evoked by rapidly moving patient’s head back and forth (“positive dynamic visual acuity test”)
    • Eyes make observable contraversive binocular saccades as head is moved
    • Tip: binocular symmetric dysfunction of semicircular canals or otoliths does not cause nystagmus
  • Unilateral or binocular dysfunction of pons or medulla
    • Fixed ocular tilt reaction, which causes persistent skew deviation
  • Unilateral or binocular dysfunction of thalamus or midbrain
    • Fixed ocular tilt reaction, which causes persistent skew deviation
    • Oscillating ocular tilt reaction, which causes seesaw nystagmus or paroxysmal skew deviation
