Vergence Pathway

Video Overview
    Video

What is it?
  • Brain pathway that initiates convergence eye movements
  • Tip: divergence eye movements may occur by relaxation of convergence
  • Adjusts position of eyes to maintain bifoveal fixation as visual target moves closer or farther away from viewer

How does it work?
  • Signal travels to occipital cortex in both cerebral hemispheres, which activates or inhibits convergence to avoid diplopia
  • From visual cortex, signal goes to tegmental midbrain area called “vergence center,” probably located near medial rectus subnuclei on both sides
  • Signal goes to medial rectus subnuclei to initiate or inhibit convergence

How does it go wrong?
  • Dysfunction of anterior visual pathway on both sides
    • Impairs transmission to occipital lobes
    • May produce comitant esotropia or exotropia (with preserved ocular ductions)
    • Common causes: any lesions of retinocortical visual pathway
  • Dysfunction of descending cerebral pathways from occipital lobes on both sides
    • Impairs transmission from occipital lobes to brainstem vergence center
    • May produce comitant esotropia or exotropia (with preserved ocular ductions)
    • Common causes: head trauma, encephalitis, narcotic or other neurotropic medication or drug use, sleep disturbance, fever, and increased intracranial pressure
  • Dysfunction of thalamus or midbrain
    • Impairs transmission within brainstem convergence center
    • May produce comitant esotropia or exotropia (and sometimes also impairment of horizontal and vertical gaze)
