What is it?
  • Three-neuron pathway that mediates pupil dilation

How does it work?
  • First-order neuron
    • Lies in hypothalamus,
      sends axons through brainstem and upper spinal cord, synapsing in intermediolateral cell column in first thoracic cord segment
  • Second-order neuron
    • Lies in spinal cord, sends signal through paraspinal region and lung apex, synapsing on superior cervical ganglion at bifurcation of common carotid artery
  • Third-order neuron
    • Lies in superior cervical ganglion,
      sends axons along wall of internal carotid artery, passing along middle ear promontory, carotid canal, cavernous sinus, and orbit, where it travels with nasociliary nerve and long ciliary nerves to iris dilator muscle (and upper lid elevator Muller muscle)

How does it go wrong?
  • Lesion of first-order neuron
    • Causes ipsilateral ptosis, miosis, and hemibody hypohydrosis (Horner syndrome), but always with other neurologic manifestations that depend on location of lesion
  • Lesion of second-order neuron
    • Causes ipsilateral ptosis, miosis, and facial hypohydrosis (Horner syndrome), often without other neurologic manifestations
  • Lesion of third-order neuron
    • Causes ipsilateral ptosis and miosis, but no hypohydrosis (incomplete Horner syndrome), as sweat fibers split off at carotid bifurcation to travel with external carotid artery
    • Tip: Horner syndrome caused by carotid artery dissection may be isolated finding or accompanied by neck or face pain
    • Trap: prbital lesions do not produce Horner syndrome!
