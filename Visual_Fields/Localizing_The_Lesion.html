Visual Field Examination

Localizing The Lesion

    • Video Overview
      Video

    What is it?
    • Determining the location of the lesion by analyzing the pattern of visual field defects

    What should you do?
    • Step 1: look at clusters of high-threshold points and try to determine if they suggest non-localizing defects, nerve fiber bundle defects, or hemianopic defects
    • Step 2: if you think there is a nerve fiber bundle defect, decide what kind
      • Central or centrocecal scotomas
        • Damage to the papillomacular bundle
        • Usually caused by optic nerve lesion, but can also be caused by macular lesion
      • Arcuate or altitudinal scotomas
        • Damage to the bundles that originate in temporal retina and arch over papillomacular bundles to enter superior and inferior poles of optic disc
        • Usually caused by optic nerve lesions, but could also be caused by inner retinal lesions
      • Temporal wedge scotomas
        • Damage to the retinal bundles that originate in the nasal retina and travel radially into the nasal portion of the optic disc
        • Usually caused by congenital optic nerve dysplasia
    • Step 3: if you think there is a hemianopic defect, decide what kind
      • Bitemporal hemianopia
        • Temporal hemianopic defects in both eyes
        • Caused by optic chiasm lesions, usually masses
      • Unilateral temporal hemianopia in one eye and normal visual field in the other eye
        • Damage to optic nerve as it approaches optic chiasm
        • Usually caused by mass lesion or inflammation
      • Temporal hemianopia in one eye and nerve fiber bundle defect in other eye
        • Damage to optic nerve at its junction with optic chiasm
        • Usually caused by mass lesion
      • Complete homonymous hemianopia
        • Damage anywhere within retrochiasmal visual pathway
        • Usually caused by stroke, but also tumor
      • Incomplete homonymous hemianopia
        • Damage in retrochiasmal visual pathway with lesion location dependent on defect shape
        • Incongruous homonymous hemianopia
          • Defects not of different size and depth in two eyes
          • Caused by optic tract lesion, usually mass
        • Congruous homonymous hemianopia
          • Defects of same size and depth in two eyes
          • Caused by posterior optic radiations or visual cortex lesions, usually masses
        • “Pie-in-the-sky” defects
          • One border aligned to vertical meridian and other border extending radially into superior visual field
          • Caused by anterior temporal lobe (Meyer’s loop) lesions, usually temporal lobectomy
        • Superior homonymous quadrantanopia
          • Fefect borders aligned to vertical and horizontal meridians in superior visual field
          • Caused by inferior primary visual cortex lesions, usually strokes
        • Inferior homonymous quadrantanopia
          • Defect borders aligned to vertical and horizontal meridians in inferior visual field
          • Caused by superior primary visual cortex lesions, usually strokes
        • Homonymous paracentral scotomas
          • Defects restricted to central 10 degrees of visual field
          • Caused by posterior primary visual cortex lesions, usually strokes
        • Macular-sparing homonymous hemianopia
          • Defects sparing central 10 degrees of visual field
          • Caused by intermediate primary visual cortex lesions, usually strokes
        • Temporal-sparing homonymous hemianopia
          • Defects sparing peripheral 30 degrees of temporal field of one eye
          • Caused by intermediate and posterior visual cortex lesions, usually strokes
        • Unilateral temporal crescent defect
          • Defect confined to unpaired peripheral temporal field of one eye
          • Caused by anterior visual cortex lesions, usually strokes
