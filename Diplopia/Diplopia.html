Diplopia

Diplopia

    • What is it?
    • Seeing two copies of viewed objects
    • Caused by optical disorders (“monocular diplopia”) or eye misalignment (“binocular diplopia”)
    • Common optical causes: uncorrected refractive error, cataract
    • Common eye misalignment causes: congenital or childhood-onset vergence disturbances, lesions of the brain stem, ocular motor cranial nerves, neuromuscular transmission, or extraocular muscles

    How does it appear?
    • Monocular diplopia
      • Patient reports seeing “ghost image
      • Ghost image persists when unaffected eye is covered and disappears when affected eye views target through pinhole
      • If uncorrected refractive error is cause, ghost image disappears when refractive error is corrected
      • If corneal or lens abnormality is cause, it will be evident on slit lamp biomicroscopy, but may be subtle
    • Binocular diplopia
      • Diplopia disappears when either eye is covered
      • Trap: patients with ocular misalignment may not report diplopia if
        • They do not have adequate vision in one or both eyes
        • The image separation is very small, in which case they may report “blurred vision”
        • The image separation is very large, in which case they may be able to ignore the deviating image
        • The ocular misalignment began in early childhood, in which case they have suppressed the deviating image
        • They have diplopia but cannot communicate it

    What else looks like it?
    • Monocular diplopia of psychogenic origin, which does not disappear when affected eye views target through pinhole

    What should you do?
    • Assess whether diplopia is monocular (optical disorder) or binocular (eye misalignment) by having patient cover either eye and discovering if diplopia persists (monocular diplopia) or disappears (binocular diplopia)
    • Confirm that monocular diplopia is of optical origin by finding that the ghost image disappears when the affected eye views the target through the pinhole occluder
    • Tip: if monocular does not disappear with the pinhole occluder, it is likely of psychogenic origin
    • In binocular diplopia, assess eye movements and eye alignment to localize causative lesion (See Eye Movement Examination and Eye Alignment Examination)

    What will happen?
    • Optical causes of monocular diplopia can be inferred by results of the pinhole examination and discoverable with refraction and slit lamp examination
    • Localization of the lesion responsible for binocular diplopia involves skillful assessment of eye movements and alignment
    • Trap: ocular misalignment causing diplopia may be present even when eye movements appear intact
    • Diplopia may be relieved with
      • Press-on (Fresnel) prisms placed on spectacles
      • Prisms ground into spectacles
      • Eye patch
      • Spectacle occluder Wrap Around Spectacle Occluder
      • Opaque contact lens inserted onto cornea Opaque Contact Lens
      • Extraocular muscle surgery
