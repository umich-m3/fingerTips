Optic Nerve And Optic Chiasm Disorders

Sphenoid Meningioma

    • What is it?
    • Tumor growing from meninges of lesser wing or body of sphenoid bone
    • Brain MRI usually diagnostic
    • Mostly middle-aged women
    • Treated with surgery and/or radiation

    What does it look like?
    • Core clinical features
      • Slowly progressive monocular or binocular visual loss
      • Reduced visual acuity
      • Nerve fiber bundle and/or hemianopic defects
      • Afferent pupil defect unless damage is equal in both eyes
      • Optic discs appear normal or pale
    • Imaging features
      • Enhancing mass based at lesser wing or body of sphenoid bone
        often inducing bone growth and even invading orbit

    What else looks like it?
    • Other masses in sellar region
    • Arachnoid cyst
    • Langerhans cell histiocytosis
    • Sphenoid sinusitis or mucocele
    • Lymphocytic hypophysitis
    • Pituitary abscess
    • Multiple sclerosis
    • Neuromyelitis optica
    • Sarcoidosis
    • Radiation damage

    What should you do?
    • Order brain MRI
    • Order maxillofacial CT to improve assessment of bone invasion or bone induction

    What will happen?
    • Tip: non-contrast CT brain may miss the tumor because tumor has same radiodensity as adjacent brain parenchyma
    • Untreated tumors may grow slowly, but growth often stops after menopause
    • Endonasal or transcranial surgery is advised if
      • Vision attributable to tumor is declining
      • Mass is large or enlarging
      • Surgical risk is acceptable
    • Expect skilled surgery to improve or stabilize vision in 75%, worsen vision in less than 25%
    • Trap: large and invasive lesions pose extra surgical hazards and often cannot be entirely excised without harming vision
    • Trap: if tumor has invaded optic canal, a feature not easily evident on imaging, prognosis for post-operative visual recovery is less favorable
    • Radiation therapy is preferred over surgery to stabilize vision if surgical risk high or surgery leaves large residual or recurring tumor
    • Trap: radiation therapy does not improve vision, may rarely worsen it after months to years, and may pose long-term risks of stroke, hypopituitarism, dementia, vasculopathy, and development of second intracranial tumor
