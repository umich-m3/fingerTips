Optic Nerve And Optic Chiasm Disorders

Typical Optic Neuritis

    • What is it?
    • Vision loss caused by demyelination of optic nerve
    • Idiopathic or part of multiple sclerosis (MS)
    • No evidence of other pertinent systemic disease
    • Afferent pupil defect may be only objective finding

    What does it look like?
    • Core features
      • Acute or subacute visual loss—usually in one eye
      • Periocular pain in affected eye worsened by eye movement (90%)
      • Reduced visual acuity and/or nerve fiber bundle visual field defects in affected eye
      • Trap: visual acuity may be 20/20 or better and visual field defects may be extremely subtle
      • Afferent pupil defect in affected eye unless fellow eye has equivalent optic nerve damage
      • Normal or minimally elevated optic disc and no other optic fundus abnormalities
    • Possible accompanying features
      • Clinical manifestations of MS
      • Previous diagnosis of MS
      • MRI enhancement of affected optic nerve (80%)
      • Characteristic high T2/FLAIR focal MRI signal abnormalities in cerebral white matter, often oriented perpendicular to long axis of lateral ventricle and in corpus callosum (50%)
      • Cerebrospinal fluid lymphocytic pleiocytosis, elevated immunoglobulins, oligoclonal bands, and/or myelin basic protein
      • Prolonged latencies in visual evoked potentials
      • Retinal nerve fiber layer thinning on optical coherence tomography in chronic condition

    What else looks like it?
    • Atypical optic neuritis
    • Ischemic optic neuropathy
    • Compressive optic neuropathy
    • Infiltrative (neoplastic) optic neuropathy
    • Leber hereditary optic neuropathy
    • Leber hereditary optic neuropathy
    • Radiation-induced optic neuropathy

    What should you do?
    • Order orbit-based MRI
    • Order lumbar puncture only if MRI is not conclusive for MS
    • Offer intravenous methylprednisolone 1 gm/day for 3 days or equivalent oral doses, followed by prednisone 1 mg/kg for 11 days, but…
    • Offer intravenous methylprednisolone 1 gm/day for 3 days or equivalent oral doses, followed by prednisone 1 mg/kg for 11 days, but…
    • Consider prophylactic disease-modifying treatment for patients who have white matter MRI abnormalities or other clinical features of MS
    • Tip: visual evoked potential testing and optical coherence tomography have limited diagnostic value in acute setting

    What will happen?
    • Substantial visual recovery of vision commonly occurs within 6 months with or without corticosteroid treatment, but…
    • Subtle visual deficits and afferent pupil defect commonly persist (80%)
    • Recurrent optic neuritis may occur in previously affected or fellow eye within 5 years (30%)
    • Clinically definite MS will commonly develop within 15 years in patients with at least one typical MRI white matter lesion noted at onset of optic neuritis (70%)
    • Clinically-definite MS will uncommonly develop within 15 years in patients with normal MRI at onset of optic neuritis (25%)
    • Severe long-term visual or neurological impairments uncommonly develop (10%)
