Optic Nerve And Optic Chiasm Disorders

Craniopharyngioma

    • What is it?
    • Congenital tumorous remnant of pharyngeal epithelium centered above sella turcica
    • Diagnosis usually made in childhood, but adults also at risk
    • Adamantinomatous tumors affect children, papillary tumors affect adults
    • Brain MRI shows distinctive features
    • Surgical resection used to confirm histology and reduce tumor bulk
    • Radiation therapy used to arrest further growth
    • Trap: recurrent acute visual loss from tumor cyst formation occurs frequently and must be treated promptly with surgery to prevent permanent vision loss

    What does it look like?
    • Core clinical features
      • Reduced visual acuity
      • Hemianopic visual field defect in at least one eye
      • Afferent pupil defect
      • Optic disc appears normal, small, or pale
    • Possible accompanying clinical features
      • Headache, hypopituitarism (especially growth retardation), papilledema, ventriculomegaly most common in children
      • Hypopituitarism most common in adults
    • Imaging features
      • Epicenter of mass is suprasellar, distinct from pituitary gland below
      • Heterogeneous signal intensity, often with cysts
      • Calcification evident on CT in children

    What else looks like it?
    • Rathke cleft cyst, which has same embryologic origin but thinner walls than craniopharyngioma
    • Other masses in sellar region
    • Arachnoid cyst
    • Epidermoid cyst
    • Langerhans cell histiocytosis
    • Sphenoid sinusitis or mucocele
    • Lymphocytic hypophysitis
    • Pituitary abscess
    • Multiple sclerosis
    • Neuromyelitis optica
    • Sarcoidosis
    • Radiation damage

    What should you do?
    • Order MRI, which usually shows distinctive features
    • Order CT, which often shows tumor calcium in children but not in adults
    • Order pituitary hormone tests
    • Refer to neurosurgeon experienced in craniopharyngioma surgery

    What will happen?
    • If untreated, progressive vision loss is common
    • Surgery confirms histology and reduces tumor burden so that radiation therapy can be more effective
    • Trap: aggressive tumor resection often causes permanent loss of vision
    • Ventriculoperitoneal shunt necessary if tumor obstructs ventricular outflow
    • High radiation total dose (>5000 cGy) necessary to prevent further tumor growth, butâ€¦
    • Trap: radiation therapy may not prevent recurrent cyst formation and may eventually damage optic nerves/optic chiasm, cause encephalopathy, vasculopathy, and hypothalamic/pituitary dysfunction (see Radiation-induced Optic Neuropathy)
    • Endoscopic surgery often effective in relieving recurrent visual loss from cyst expansion
      but may have to be performed repeatedly
    • Tip: advise patients with known craniopharyngiomas to contact vision care provider promptly if vision loss occurs, as earlier decompression prevents permanent vision loss from tumor cyst expansion
    • Hypopituitarism requires intensive endocrinologic care
