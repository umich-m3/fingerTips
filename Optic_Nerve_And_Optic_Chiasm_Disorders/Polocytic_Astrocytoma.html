Optic Nerve And Optic Chiasm Disorders

Pilocytic Astrocytoma (Optic Glioma)

    • What is it?
    • Congenital low-grade (WHO Grade 1) astrocytoma of optic nerves, optic chiasm, hypothalamus (“optic glioma”)
    • Usually discovered before age 20 years
    • Neurofibromatosis type 1 present in 30–50%
    • MRI shows intrinsic mass in optic nerve, optic chiasm, and/or hypothalamus
    • No biopsy necessary if imaging distinctive
    • Chemotherapy often used but benefit uncertain as controlled trials have not been performed
    • Surgical resection of optic nerve reserved for unsightly proptosis when eye has no light perception

    What does it look like?
    • Core features
      • Stationary or progressive visual loss in one eye or both 
      • Reduced visual acuity and visual field defects in one or both eyes
      • Afferent pupil defect
      • Optic discs appear normal or pale (elevated if orbital tumor invades distal optic nerve) 
    • Possible accompanying features
      • Monocular pendular nystagmus (usually vertical) Seesaw nystagmus [VIDEO Seesaw Nystagmus from Video Supranuclear Ocular Motor Pathways Part 5: Vestibulo-Ocular Pathway]
      • Proptosis (if large tumor in orbit)
      • Iris hamartomas called “Lisch nodules” (signs of neurofibromatosis Type 1) if patient aged above 10 years, less likely earlier
      • Eyelid plexiform neuroma
    • Brain/orbit MRI shows enlargement or signal alteration within optic nerve, optic chiasm, and/or hypothalamus
      • Tip: pilocytic astrocytoma is unusual among optic nerve lesions in causing marked enlargement of the nerve and/or chiasm together with avid enhancement

    What else looks like it?
    • Other orbital and sellar region tumors
    • Optic neuritis
    • Neuromyelitis optica
    • Lymphocytic hypophysitis
    • Sarcoidosis
    • Metastatic cancer
    • Hematopoietic cancers, including lymphoma
    • Langerhans histiocytosis

    What should you do?
    • Order orbit-based MRI, usually diagnostic
    • Biopsy lesion only if imaging features are not classic or large exophytic component present

    What will happen?
    • Tumor size and visual function often remain stable in untreated patients
    • Visual decline may be caused either by tumor growth, tumor production of extracellular matrix, or reactive meningeal hyperplasia 
    • Intraorbital tumors may have intracranial component, but they do not spread intracranially
    • Trap: do not treat tumor apparently confined to orbit, except when there is disfiguring proptosis and a blind eye, in which case excision of intraorbital optic nerve, sparing eye and extraocular muscles, may be indicated
    • Place ventriculoperitoneal shunt for obstructive hydrocephalus
    • Prescribe chemotherapy for patients under age 9 whose tumors are growing or involve optic chiasm and/or hypothalamus, and are causing severe or worsening vision or hypothalamic dysfunction
    • Consider radiation therapy as alternative to chemotherapy for patients older than 9 years
    • Trap: radiation therapy in patients with NF 1 carries high risk of later occlusive vasculopathy and secondary tumors
    • No controlled trials to show that any treatment improves on natural history
    • These tumors may rarely cause severe neuroendocrine morbidity or death
