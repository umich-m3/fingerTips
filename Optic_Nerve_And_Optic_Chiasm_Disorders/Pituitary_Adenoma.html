Optic Nerve And Optic Chiasm Disorders

Pituitary Adenoma

    • What is it?
    • Tumor caused by hyperplasia of anterior pituitary gland
    • Young and middle-aged women most affected
    • Visual loss usually slow, but may be acute from tumor hemorrhage (“pituitary apoplexy”)
    • Most tumors are endocrinologically silent 
    • Brain MRI often diagnostic, but…
    • Trap: other masses can mimic adenoma on MRI 
    • Transsphenoidal surgery usually provides dramatic visual improvement, but… 
    • Large extrasellar extension may pose high surgical risk
    • Marked pre-operative vision impairment adversely affects visual outcome

    What does it look like?
    • Core features
      • Subacute or chronic visual loss, often monocular
      • Sudden visual loss usually means tumor hemorrhage in “pituitary apoplexy”
      • Hemianopic defect
      • Afferent pupil defect
      • Optic discs appear normal or pale
    • Possible accompanying features
      • Chronic or new headache
      • Hormonally-silent tumors constitute 70% and are typically larger and more vision-impairing at presentation than these hormonally-active tumors
        • Prolactin-secreting: galactorrhea, amenorrhea, decreased libido, impotence
        • Growth hormone-secreting: acromegaly, gigantism
        • Corticotrophin-secreting: cushingoid features, diabetes, myopathy
        • Thyrotropin-secreting: hyperthyroidism, goiter
        • Gonadotropin-secreting: precocious puberty, decreased libido

    What else looks like it?
    • Other masses in sellar region
    • Arachnoid cyst
    • Epidermoid cyst
    • Langerhans cell histiocytosis
    • Sphenoid sinusitis or mucocele
    • Lymphocytic hypophysitis
    • Pituitary abscess
    • Multiple sclerosis
    • Neuromyelitis optica
    • Sarcoidosis
    • Radiation damage

    What should you do?
    • Order brain MRI, which shows mass expanding pituitary gland and sometimes extending upwards through diaphragma sellae (“dumbbell,” “snowman”), laterally into cavernous sinus, or inferiorly into sphenoid sinus; displacement of optic chiasm by tumor associated with visual field loss
    • Trap: common MRI mimics of pituitary adenoma are pituitary hyperplasia, craniopharyngioma, metastasis, germ cell tumor
    • Order serum prolactin: >200ng/ml signals prolactinoma, usually treated first with dopamine agonist (cabergoline
    • Refer non-prolactinomas to neurosurgeon

    What will happen?
    • Prolactinomas often shrink rapidly with dramatic visual improvement following dopamine agonist (cabergoline) therapy, making surgery unnecessary, but…
    • Trap: dopamine agonist therapy not always effective or tolerated and must be maintained indefinitely
    • Non-prolactinomas effectively managed by transsphenoidal or wide endonasal surgery with visual improvement or stabilization in >90%
    • If tumor must be approached transcranially, visual improvement less likely and chance of visual damage more likely
    • Regrowth of tumor uncommon but may occur many years after initial treatment, so must maintain imaging and clinical surveillance
    • Radiation therapy reserved for non-candidates for surgery, large post-operative residual tumor or tumor regrowth
