Transient Vision Loss

Transient Monocular Vision Loss

    • What is it?

    • Abrupt loss of vision in one eye lasting from seconds to hours
    • Produced by diminished blood flow to the eye
    • Common causes
      • Systemic hypotension
      • Reduced cardiac output
      • Embolism originating from a stenotic cervical carotid artery
      • Atrial fibrillation
      • Cardiac valve or mural thrombus
      • Retinal vein occlusion
      • Vasospasm of retinal arterioles
      • Hyperviscosity/hypercoagulable states
      • Optic disc edema (including papilledema)
      • Demyelinating optic neuropathy

    How does it appear?

    • Blank, fuzzy, dark, bright, or flickering area in the visual field of one eye
    • Trap: patients with transient binocular (homonymous) visual loss often report that the deficit affected only one eye—the eye with the temporal hemianopia!
    • Visual loss provoked by sitting or standing = reduced cardiac output, systemic hypotension, optic disc edema (especially papilledema)
    • Visual loss provoked by exercise = demyelinating optic neuropathy

    What else looks like it?

    • Mucus debris in the tear film (usually clears with blinking)
    • Corneal edema from endothelial malfunction (usually worse on awakening)

    What to do?

    • Elicit features suggesting that transient vision loss was monocular
      • Vision was undisturbed when the patient closed the affected eye
      • Visual loss primarily involved the nasal visual field
      • Vision loss did not include expanding zigzags, waves, silver, broken glass, or heat waves
    • Perform ophthalmoscopy to look for
      • Papilledema
      • Retinal embolus
      • Venous stasis retinopathy
    • Perform formal visual field examination (to detect a lingering deficit)
    • Measure blood pressure (looking for systemic hypertension or hypotension)
    • Send the patient for prompt investigation of the underlying cause with a latency based on how recent was the symptom (the more recent, the quicker the referral)

    What will happen?

    • Ophthalmic examination is often normal
    • High or low systemic blood pressure will often explain transient vision loss
    • Work-up includes
      • Acute phase reactants (for giant cell arteritis)
      • Blood count and protein electrophoresis (for hypercoagulable state)
      • Cervical carotid ultrasound or CT and CTA (for cervical carotid artery stenosis/dissection)
        • Bifurcational stenosis by 50% of more
          often prompts endarterectomy, although the evidence is not robust that it reduces eye or brain stroke in isolated transient monocular vision loss
        • Trap: bifurcational stenosis of less than 50% does not justify endarterectomy
      • Echocardiogram (for irregular heart wall motion or valvular disease)
      • Brain MRI (for recent stroke)
      • Heart rhythm monitoring (for atrial fibrillation)
    • If no explanation is found in older adults, conventional stroke risk reduction measures are recommended
    • If no explanation is found in young patients, retinal vasospasm may be considered as the cause and treated with calcium channel blockers, but only if the attacks are frequent
