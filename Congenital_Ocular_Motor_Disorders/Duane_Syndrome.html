Congenital Ocular Motor Disorders

Duane Syndrome

    • What is it?
    • Congenital malformation of sixth nerve nucleus with disturbed innervation of extraocular muscles
    • Absence of axons destined for the lateral rectus muscle with preservation of axons destined for the medial longitudinal fasciculus
    • Axons from the ipsilateral third nerve nucleus sprout into the sixth nerve to innervate lateral rectus muscle (“dysinnervation,” “miswiring”)
    • Pattern of misalignment varies according to type of miswiring

    What does it look like?
    • Diplopia is not prominent—it is intermittent or even absent
    • Face turn toward the lesioned side is common
    • Duane syndrome Type 1 Duane Type 1
      • Abduction deficit
      • Esotropia
      • Narrowing of palpebral fissure height with adduction of the affected eye
      • Upshoot or downshoot of adducting eye (sometimes)
    • Duane syndrome Type 2
      • Adduction deficit
      • Exotropia
    • Duane syndrome Type 3 Duane Type 3
      • Abduction and adduction deficits
      • Esotropia in ipsilateral gaze, exotropia in contralateral gaze

    What else looks like it?
    • Acquired sixth nerve palsy
    • Orbital restrictive syndrome
    • Myasthenia gravis
    • Spasm of near reflex
    • Tip: most patients with these four acquired causes of an abduction deficit will report diplopia; patients with Duane syndrome rarely report diplopia, probably because the lesion is congenital and they have suppressed the image from the deviating eye

    What should you do?
    • Look for narrowing of the palpebral fissure on adduction of affected eye and for upshoot or downshoot of adducting eye
    • Tip: ask to look at childhood photographs, which may disclose face turn and/or eye misalignment

    What will happen?
    • Extraocular muscle surgery indicated for face turn
    • Trap: patients with Duane syndrome are often misdiagnosed as having an acquired abduction deficit, triggering unnecessary investigations and inappropriate work waivers
