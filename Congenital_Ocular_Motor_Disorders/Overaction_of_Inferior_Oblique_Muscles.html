Congenital Ocular Motor Disorders

Overaction of Inferior Oblique Muscles

    • What is it?
    • Congenital eye movement abnormality involving excessive upward movement of adducted eye
    • Usually affects both eyes
    • Attributed to abnormal attachment of extraocular muscles
    • Causes vertical misalignment but no diplopia

    What does it look like?
    • Adducting eye “rolls” upward so that it becomes higher than the abducting eye
    • Vertical misalignment on eccentric horizontal gaze
    • Exodeviation predominantly in upgaze (“V pattern exotropia”)

    What else looks like it?
    • Superior oblique palsy
    • Orbital restrictive syndrome limiting supraduction in abducted eye

    What should you do?
    • Trap: avoid misdiagnosis of superior oblique palsy unless patients report diplopia and the hyperdeviation fulfills “three-step test” (See Fourth Nerve Palsy)
    • Trap: avoid misdiagnosis of orbital restrictive syndrome limiting supraduction if patient does not have diplopia
    • Tip: when performing eye muscle surgery on patients with inferior oblique overaction, consider “V pattern” in repositioning of extraocular muscles

    What will happen?
    • Patients do not have diplopia, so the main issue is the blemish of an upward drift of affected eye
    • Surgical weakening of superior rectus muscle in affected eye may be effective in reducing upward drift
    • Trap: patients are often misdiagnosed as having a condition causing acquired ocular misalignment and subjected to unnecessary investigations
