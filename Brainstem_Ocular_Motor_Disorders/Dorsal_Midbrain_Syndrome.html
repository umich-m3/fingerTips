Brainstem Ocular Motor Disorders

Dorsal Midbrain Syndrome

    • What is it?
    • Impaired binocular upgaze and other neuro-ophthalmic deficits
    • Caused by dysfunction of the dorsal midbrain
    • Common causes: thalamic or midbrain stroke/hemorrhage, pineal region tumor, dorsal midbrain tumor, aqueductal stenosis, failed ventriculo-peritoneal shunt
    • Uncommon causes: demyelination, head trauma

    What does it look like?
    • Core clinical features
      • Reduced upgaze Upgaze Paresis in Dorsal Midbrain Syndrome
      • Trap: reduced upgaze is often overlooked because it is mild or the patient avoids looking up Subtle Upgaze Dysfunction
      • Convergence–retraction of the eyes with attempted upgaze, especially in pursuing a downgoing optokinetic strip Convergence-Retraction in Dorsal Midbrain
      • Anisocoria with large pupils that have reduced constriction to light but intact constriction to a near target (“tectal light-near dissociation”) Tectal Pupils
      • Bilateral lid retraction (“Collier sign”) Lid Retracion in Dorsal Midbrain Stroke
    • Possible accompanying neuro-ophthalmic features
      • Reduced downgaze Lid Retracion in Dorsal Midbrain Stroke
      • Downwardly deviated eyes (“sunset eyes”) Dorsal Midbrain Syndrome With Shunt Malfunction
      • Exotropia, esotropia or hypertropia
      • Torsional misalignment (fourth nerve palsy)
      • Torsional nystagmus
      • Skew deviation
      • Papilledema (if the Sylvian aqueduct is blocked)
    • Imaging features
      • MRI usually shows a lesion in or near the dorsal midbrain
        pineal tumor,
        thalamic hemorrhage,
        or obstructive hydrocephalus from aqueductal stenosis

    What else looks like it?
    • Myasthenia gravis
    • Graves disease
    • Bilateral orbital trauma
    • Progressive supranuclear palsy
    • Whipple disease

    What should you do?
    • Look for a constellation of features typical of dorsal midbrain syndrome, especially reduced upgaze
    • Tip: reduced upgaze is rarely an isolated abnormality in dorsal midbrain syndrome
    • Exclude shunt failure in patients with shunted hydrocephalus
    • Consider pineal and tectal midbrain tumor, aqueductal stenosis, and demyelination in children and young adults
    • Consider thalamic stroke, thalamic hemorrhage, and demyelination in older adults
    • Tip: remember that fourth nerve palsy (often bilateral) and dorsal midbrain syndrome are common in severe blunt head trauma because a moving midbrain smashes into the stationary sharp edge of the tentorium cerebell
    • Order brain MRI

    What will happen?
    • Clinical manifestations often lessen or disappear within weeks in mild stroke, demyelination, treated aqueductal stenosis and shunt malfunction, but…
    • Manifestations are often permanent following compressive pineal region lesions, stroke, and head trauma
    • Diplopia may be relieved with
      • Ground-in or press-on spectacle prisms
      • Eye patch
      • Spectacle occluder Wrap Acound Spectacle Occluder
      • Opaque contact lens Opaque Contact Lens
      • Extraocular muscle surgery
