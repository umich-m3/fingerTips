Monocular Pendular Nystagmus of Childhood

    • What is it?
    • Monocular pendular nystagmus identified within first decade of life
    • Usually limited to vertical plane
    • Common causes: anterior visual pathway tumor (pilocytic astrocytoma, craniopharyngioma)
    • Uncommon cause: optic nerve hypoplasia

    What does it look like?
    • Core clinical features
      • Monocular pendular nystagmus, usually vertical Monocular Vertical Pendular Nystagmus
      • Subnormal visual acuity in oscillating eye
      • Afferent pupil defect in oscillating eye
      • Pale or small optic disc in oscillating eye
      • Anisometropia, strabismus, or media opacity in oscillating eye sometimes
      • MRI may show anterior visual pathway tumor

    What else looks like it?
    • Spasms nutans
    • Infantile motor nystagmus
    • Infantile sensory nystagmus
    • Acquired binocular pendular nystagmus

    What should you do?
    • If no ocular cause is found, order brain MRI
    • If afferent pupil defect is present, suspect anterior visual pathway lesion, especially tumor

    What will happen?
    • MRI may identify a tumor with enough specific features to make biopsy unnecessary
    • Pilocytic astrocytoma (“optic glioma”) is treatable with chemotherapy, although there are no controlled trials to establish benefit
    • There is no effective treatment for the nystagmus, but it may improve if vision improves
