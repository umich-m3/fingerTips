Nystagmus

Nystagmus Overview

    • What is it?
    • Involuntary rhythmic oscillation of the eyes
    • Two forms: pendular and jerk
    • May be physiologic if it is a jerk nystagmus that is small in amplitude, unsustained, and limited to the extremes of horizontal gaze
    • Common underlying mechanisms of pathologic nystagmus: vestibular imbalance, defective eccentric gaze-holding, poor vision, disturbed conjugate yoking
    • Common causes of pendular nystagmus: brainstem or cerebellar disorder, poor vision, idiopathic congenital disorder
    • Common causes of pathologic jerk nystagmus: idiopathic congenital disorder, medications, acute peripheral vestibulopathy, brainstem or cerebellar disorder

    What does it look like?
    • Patient may report blurred vision but not illusory movement of viewed objects (“oscillopsia”)
    • Features of physiologic nystagmus
      • Horizontal or horizontal-rotary jerk nystagmus limited to extremes of horizontal gaze
      • Equal amplitude in both extremes of horizontal gaze
      • No more than 3 beats
      • No accompanying ocular motor or other pertinent neurologic manifestations Physiologic sidebeat nystagmus
    • Features of pathologic nystagmus
      • Usually involve both eyes, but may be of different amplitude in the two eyes, and may rarely be limited to one eye
      • Tip: the most common form of monocular nystagmus is the abducting jerk nystagmus of internuclear ophthalmoplegia Internuclear Opthalmoplegia with Exotropia
      • Tip: two forms of binocular nystagmus are so asymmetric that they appear to be limited to one eye
        • Acquired vertical pendular nystagmus of childhood, which reflects ipsilateral optic neuropathy, often pilocytic astrocytoma Monocular Pendular Nystagmus of Childhood
        • Spasmus nutans: high frequency low amplitude horizontal nystagmus, often accompanied by head nodding and truncal titubation; its cause is unknown Spasms Nutants
      • Oscillations begin with a slow conjugate drift, and are followed by an oppositely-directed slow conjugate drift (pendular nystagmus) Acquired Pendular Nystagmus Fragment or an oppositely-directed rapid conjugate movement (jerk nystagmus) Sidebeat Nystagmus Fragment
      • Tip: in infantile motor or sensory nystagmus, it is difficult to tell if you are observing a pendular or a jerk nystagmus because of the odd waveform Infantile Motor Nystagmus
      • Oscillations may have a trajectory that is horizontal, vertical, torsional (rotary), or mixed
      • Tip: a pure rotary jerk nystagmus is ALWAYS caused by a BRAINSTEM or DIENCEPHALIC DISORDER; a mixed horizontal-rotary jerk nystagmus may be caused by a PERIPHERAL VESTIBULAR DISORDER or a BRAINSTEM or DIENCEPHALIC DISORDER
      • Tip: a mixed horizontal and vertical pendular nystagmus will have a circular or oval trajectory and look like a windmill or eggbeater! Acquired pendular Nystagmus Fragment
      • Both eyes usually move in the same plane, with one exception
        • Seesaw nystagmus: both eyes have conjugate torsional movements, but one eye moves up while the other eye moves down Seesaw Nystagmus
      • Both eyes usually move in the same direction, with one exception
        • Oculomasticatory myorhythmia: eyes converge and diverge synchronously with spasms of the masticatory muscles, a condition virtually diagnostic of Whipple disease
      • Oscillation amplitude and frequency often vary from one gaze position to another, and may be high or low; a high amplitude is usually associated with a low frequency and a low amplitude with a high frequency
      • Fast corrective phase of horizontal and vertical jerk nystagmus is usually in the direction of gaze, with two exceptions
        • Infantile motor nystagmus and infantile sensory nystagmus: the nystagmus plane remains HORIZONTAL even in upgaze and downgaze Infantile Motor Nystagmus
        • Acute peripheral vestibulopathy: the fast phase of horizontal or horizontal-rotary jerk nystagmus may remain IN ONE DIRECTION, no matter the position of side gaze Acute Peripheral Vestibulopathy
      • Fast corrective phase of torsional (rotary) jerk nystagmus is usually clockwise in left gaze and counter clockwise in right gaze, with one exception
        • Acute medullary lesion: the fast phase of pure torsional (rotary) nystagmus may be clockwise in right gaze and counter clockwise in left gaze Torsional Nystagmus in Dorsolateral
    • Unusual Features
      • Oscillation amplitude may increase when either eye is covered (“latent nystagmus") Latent Nystagmus
      • Oscillations may occur synchronously in the eyes and in the palate or other branchial arch-derived muscles Oculopatal Tremor
      • In primary gaze position, the direction of the fast corrective phase of horizontal jerk nystagmus may switch in 2-minute cycles (“periodic alternating nystagmus”) Periodic Alternating Nystagmus
      • Oscillations be disconjugate (convergent) and accompanied by synchronous jaw oscillations (“oculomasticatory myorhythmia”)

    What else looks like it?
    • Superior oblique myokymia: look for nonrhythmic monocular torsional saccades synchronous with a report of a brief cramp in the affected eye, blurred vision, or diplopia Superior Oblique Myokymia
    • Square wave jerks: look for binocular unidirectional horizontal saccades followed by a brief interval and then an oppositely-directed saccade that returns the eyes to primary gaze position Square Wave Jerks
    • Square wave jerks: look for binocular unidirectional horizontal saccades followed by a brief interval and then an oppositely-directed saccade that returns the eyes to primary gaze position Ocular Flutter
    • Opsoclonus: look for binocular rapid back-to-back horizontal, vertical, and oblique saccades Opsoclonu
    • Saccades of inattention: look for conjugate saccades in any direction that interrupt fixation in the setting of deep thought or distracted state Fixation stability
    • Ocular dysmetria: look for back-to-back saccades of decreasing amplitude as the eyes settle on a newly fixated target Ocular Dysmetria
    • Ocular bobbing: look for conjugate vertical ocular movements with at least one very slow phase in a comatose patient Ocular bobbing
    • Ping pong gaze: look for slow horizontal conjugate movements that take the eyes from one extreme of gaze to the other in a comatose patient

    What should you do?
    • Step 1: distinguish nystagmus from its imitators, which are NOT initiated by a slow conjugate drift of the eyes, except for ocular bobbing and ping pong gaze, which may have an initial slow conjugate drift but always occur in comatose patients
    • Step 2: distinguish physiologic nystagmus from pathologic nystagmus
    • Step 3: distinguish infantile (congenital) forms of nystagmus from acquired forms of nystagmus by noting that infantile nystagmus has oscillations that
      • Begin within the first six months of life
      • Are almost always in the HORIZONTAL plane
      • Remain in the horizontal plane even when the eyes are directed into upgaze or downgaze
      • Appear to have a hybrid pendular-jerk waveform (and prove to have an increasing velocity exponential slow phase on eye movement recordings)
      • Have reduced amplitude in one eccentric position of gaze (“null zone”)
      • May be accompanied by a face turn
      • May have a latent component
      • May have accompanying esotropia
      • May have accompanying dissociated vertical deviation
      • May have an underlying cause of impaired vision (optic neuropathy, retinopathy, amblyopia)
      • May have head tilt and head titubation but no truncal ataxia
    • Step 4: distinguish one form of acquired nystagmus from another by observing the features of the nystagmus and its accompanying features

    What deserves your special attention?
    • These forms of nystagmus have special diagnostic value
      • Monocular abducting nystagmus: usually internuclear ophthalmoplegia, but can be myasthenia gravis
      • Seesaw nystagmus: diencephalic lesion, congenital or acquired
      • Downbeat nystagmus: medullocerebellar dysfunction, structural or metabolic
      • Upbeat nystagmus: diencephalic, brainstem, or cerebellar dysfunction, usually acquired
      • Periodic alternating nystagmus: medullocerebellar dysfunction, usually congenital
      • Oculopalatal tremor: acquired lesion in Guillain-Mollaret triangle between dentate, olivary, and red nuclei, usually occurring months after a brainstem lesion
      • Horizontal nystagmus in primary gaze that remains horizontal in upgaze and downgaze (“uniplanar”): ALWAYS congenital
      • Latent nystagmus: ALWAYS congenital
      • Purely torsional nystagmus: diencephalic or brainstem/cerebellar dysfunction, usually acquired
      • Spasmus nutans: idiopathic but self-limited
      • Oculomasticatory myorhythmia: Whipple disease
    • Tip: gaze-evoked sidebeat nystagmus with its fast phase in the direction of gaze (“direction-changing jerk nystagmus”) is the most common form of nystagmus; it may be congenital, caused by acquired lesion of brainstem, or reflect metabolic dysfunction, a paraneoplastic phenomenon, or medication toxicity Sidebeat Nystagmus
    • Tip: horizontal-rotary nystagmus with a fast phase that does not change directions in extremes of gaze (“non direction-changing”) and whose amplitude increases with gaze directed away from the side of the lesion suggests acute peripheral vestibulopathy; that diagnosis is fortified if acute vertigo, tinnitus, or hearing loss is present, there is no ataxia, and the head impulse test is positive Acute Periphereal Vestibulopathy
    • Tip: Wernicke encephalopathy can cause any form of nystagmus, but most typically side beat and upbeat jerk nystagmus; it should be suspected in any form of acquired nystagmus because treatment is effective and urgent!
