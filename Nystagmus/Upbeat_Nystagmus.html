Upbeat Nystagmus

    • What is it?

    • Jerk nystagmus with its fast phase in the upward direction
    • Reflects brainstem dysfunction anywhere from midbrain to medulla
    • Common causes: brain stem/cerebellar stroke, hemorrhage, tumors, demyelination, infections, neurodegenerative disorders, paraneoplastic disorders
    • Uncommon causes: hypomagnesemia, Wernicke encephalopathy, transient disorder in normal infants

    What does it look like?

    • Core clinical features
      • Usually no symptoms, although the patient may report blurred vision if the nystagmus amplitude is high in primary gaze position
      • Jerk nystagmus with its fast phase upward
      • Nystagmus may be present in straight ahead gaze or upgaze, less often in downgaze
    • Possible accompanying clinical features
      • Ataxia
      • Skew deviation
      • Saccadic pursuit
      • Altered consciousness
      • Extremity weakness

    What else looks like it?

    • Acquired pendular nystagmus
    • Flutter/opsoclonus
    • Ocular dysmetria

    What should you do?

    • Rule out a magnesium or vitamin deficiency state, including Wernicke encephalopathy
    • Order brain MRI/MRA
    • If brain imaging, basic laboratory studies, and lumbar puncture are unrevealing, order a paraneoplastic panel

    What will happen?

    • Depends on the underlying cause
    • Memantine, 4-aminopyridine, or baclofen treatment may improve oscillopsia, but side effects are common
    • Tip: for any jerk nystagmus apparent in extremes of upgaze, downgaze, or side gaze, think of Wernicke encephalopathy, which is eminently treatable!
