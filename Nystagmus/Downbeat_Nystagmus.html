Downbeat Nystagmus

    • What is it?
    • Jerk nystagmus with its fast phase in the downward direction
    • Associated with dysfunction of the medulla or cerebellum
    • Common causes: neurodegenerative states (including idiopathic cerebellar degeneration), multiple sclerosis, Chiari malformation, paraneoplastic disorders
    • Uncommon causes: lithium toxicity, Wernicke encephalopathy, cerebellar or medullary stroke, tumor, inflammation, transient benign disorder in normal infants

    What does it look like?
    • Viewed objects appear to be moving downward (“oscillopsia”)
    • Jerk nystagmus with its fast phase downward, most obvious when the patient looks down and to side Downbeat Nystagmus
    • Nystagmus trajectory may appear oblique because there is often an associated horizontal vector
    • Other neurologic manifestations, especially ataxia, often present

    What else looks like it?
    • Nothing, but this nystagmus is often overlooked because it is often not pronounced in straight-ahead gaze

    What should you do?
    • Rule out toxic and vitamin deficiency states, including Wernicke encephalopathy
    • Order brain MRI
    • If brain imaging is negative, order a lumbar puncture
    • If lumbar puncture is negative, order a paraneoplastic panel

    What will happen?
    • Trap: this form of nystagmus may be misdiagnosed as “ocular bobbing”
    • Surgical decompression of Chiari malformation usually does not improve the nystagmus
    • Clonazepam, chlorzoxazone, 4-aminopyridine, or 3,4-diaminopyridine treatment may reduce nystagmus amplitude and oscillopsia
